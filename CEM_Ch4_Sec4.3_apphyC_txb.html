Section 4.3: Capacitor Basics

A capacitor is a device that stores electric charge and energy. It consists of two conductors separated by an insulating material (dielectric or vacuum). When connected to a potential difference, equal and opposite charges accumulate on the plates.

Capacitance Definition:

\[ C = \frac{Q}{V} \] where \(C\) is capacitance, \(Q\) is the charge stored, and \(V\) is the potential difference.

Unit: the farad (F), where \(1 \, \text{F} = 1 \, \text{C}/\text{V}\).

Key Ideas:
  • Capacitance depends only on geometry and dielectric properties, not on \(Q\) or \(V\).
  • Capacitors are essential in circuits for storing energy, filtering signals, and managing voltage fluctuations.

Example 1

A capacitor stores a charge of \( 6 \, \mu C \) when connected to a \( 12 \, V \) battery. Find its capacitance.

Using definition of capacitance:

\[ C = \frac{Q}{V} = \frac{6 \times 10^{-6}}{12} = 5.0 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{F} = 0.5 \, \mu F \]

Thus, the capacitor has capacitance of \( 0.5 \, \mu F \).

Practice Problems

  1. Define capacitance and state its SI unit.
  2. A capacitor has capacitance of \(10 \, \mu F\). How much charge does it store at \(50 \, V\)?
  3. Explain why capacitance is independent of the actual stored charge.
  4. What physical features of a capacitor determine its capacitance?
  5. Give one practical use of capacitors in electronic circuits.
