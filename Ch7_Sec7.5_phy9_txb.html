Section 7.5: Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics is the study of energy transformations, particularly heat and work, in physical systems. It helps us understand how energy flows, how systems reach equilibrium, and how energy can be used to perform work.

Laws of Thermodynamics

  • First Law (Law of Energy Conservation): Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. Heat added to a system can increase its internal energy or do work.
    Formula: \( \Delta U = Q - W \)
    \( \Delta U \) = change in internal energy, Q = heat added, W = work done by the system.
  • Second Law: Heat flows spontaneously from high temperature to low temperature. Entropy of an isolated system never decreases. This law introduces the concept of irreversibility in natural processes.
  • Third Law: As temperature approaches absolute zero, the entropy of a perfect crystal approaches zero.

Heat Engines and Work

A heat engine converts thermal energy into mechanical work by exploiting a temperature difference. The efficiency of a heat engine is the ratio of work done to heat absorbed:

\( \eta = \frac{W}{Q_\text{in}} \times 100\% \)

Where \(W\) is work done and \(Q_\text{in}\) is heat absorbed from the hot reservoir.

Examples include steam engines, internal combustion engines, and refrigerators (operating in reverse).

Worked Examples

Example 1: A system absorbs 500 J of heat and does 200 J of work. Find the change in internal energy.

Solution: \( \Delta U = Q - W = 500 - 200 = 300 \, \text{J} \)

Example 2: A heat engine absorbs 1200 J of heat and does 360 J of work. Find its efficiency.

Solution: \( \eta = \frac{W}{Q_\text{in}} \times 100\% = \frac{360}{1200} \times 100\% = 30\% \)
