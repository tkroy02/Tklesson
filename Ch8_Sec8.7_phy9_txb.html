Section 8.7: Sound Properties (Pitch and Loudness)

Sound has two main perceptual properties: pitch and loudness. These properties are determined by the physical characteristics of the sound wave, such as frequency and amplitude.

Pitch

Pitch is how high or low a sound appears to a listener. It depends directly on the frequency of the sound wave:

  • Higher frequency (\(f\)) → higher pitch.
  • Lower frequency → lower pitch.

Example: A piano key with a frequency of 440 Hz produces the musical note A4, which is a high pitch compared to 220 Hz (A3).

Loudness

Loudness is how strong or intense a sound appears to a listener. It is related to the amplitude of the wave:

  • Larger amplitude → louder sound.
  • Smaller amplitude → softer sound.

Example: Hitting a drum softly produces a quieter sound than hitting it forcefully, even if the pitch (frequency) is the same.

Additional Notes

  • Human hearing typically ranges from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz.
  • Pitch and loudness are subjective perceptions; different listeners may perceive the same sound slightly differently.
  • Amplitude is measured in decibels (dB) — higher dB indicates louder sound.
  • Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz) — higher Hz indicates higher pitch.
