Section 10.4: Coulomb's Law with Three Charges

When more than two charges are involved, the net electrostatic force on a charge is the vector sum of the forces exerted by the other charges. Each pair of charges interacts according to Coulomb's law:

\[ F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} \]

For multiple charges, calculate each force separately and sum vectorially considering magnitude and direction.

Example 1: Net Force on \(q_1\)

Problem: Three charges \(q_1 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_2 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_3 = -1 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are placed in a straight line with 0.2 m between each. Find the net force on \(q_1\).

Solution:

Step 1: Identify forces acting on \(q_1\)

\(q_1\) experiences forces due to \(q_2\) and \(q_3\). We'll calculate each separately using Coulomb's Law:

\[ F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} \] where \(k = 9 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N\,m^2/C^2}\).

Step 2: Force on \(q_1\) due to \(q_2\)

Distance between \(q_1\) and \(q_2\): \(r = 0.2 \, \mathrm{m}\)

Substitute values:

\[ F_{12} = (9 \times 10^9) \frac{(2 \times 10^{-6})(3 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.2)^2} \] \[ F_{12} = (9 \times 10^9) \frac{6 \times 10^{-12}}{0.04} \] \[ F_{12} = 1.35 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Direction: Both charges are positive, so the force is repulsive → \(q_1\) is pushed away from \(q_2\).

Step 3: Force on \(q_1\) due to \(q_3\)

Distance between \(q_1\) and \(q_3\): \(2r = 0.4 \, \mathrm{m}\)

Substitute values:

\[ F_{13} = (9 \times 10^9) \frac{|2 \times 10^{-6} \cdot (-1 \times 10^{-6})|}{(0.4)^2} \] \[ F_{13} = (9 \times 10^9) \frac{2 \times 10^{-12}}{0.16} \] \[ F_{13} = 0.1125 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Direction: Charges are unlike (positive and negative), so the force is attractive → \(q_1\) is pulled toward \(q_3\).

Step 4: Determine net force

Assume rightward is positive. \(F_{12}\) acts to the left (repulsion from \(q_2\)), \(F_{13}\) acts to the right (attraction to \(q_3\)):

\[ F_\mathrm{net} = F_{13} - F_{12} = 0.1125 - 1.35 = -1.2375 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Step 5: Interpret the sign

Negative sign → net force is toward the left (away from \(q_2\)).

Answer: \[ F_\mathrm{net} \approx 1.24 \, \mathrm{N} \text{ to the left} \]

Example 2: Net Force on \(q_2\)

Problem: Using the same charges as Example 1 (\(q_1 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_2 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_3 = -1 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), spaced 0.2 m apart), find the net force on \(q_2\).

Visual Representation:

Figure: Forces acting on \(q_2\). Arrow lengths are illustrative of magnitude.

Solution:

Step 1: Identify forces acting on \(q_2\)

\(q_2\) experiences forces due to \(q_1\) and \(q_3\). We calculate each using Coulomb's Law:

\[ F = k \frac{|q_2 q|}{r^2}, \quad k = 9 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N\,m^2/C^2} \]

Step 2: Force on \(q_2\) due to \(q_1\)

Distance: \(r = 0.2 \, \mathrm{m}\)

\[ F_{21} = (9 \times 10^9) \frac{(3 \times 10^{-6})(2 \times 10^{-6})}{(0.2)^2} = 1.35 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Direction: Both positive → repulsive → \(q_2\) pushed to the right.

Step 3: Force on \(q_2\) due to \(q_3\)

Distance: \(r = 0.2 \, \mathrm{m}\)

\[ F_{23} = (9 \times 10^9) \frac{|3 \times 10^{-6} \cdot (-1 \times 10^{-6})|}{(0.2)^2} = 0.675 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Direction: Positive and negative → attractive → \(q_2\) pulled to the right toward \(q_3\).

Step 4: Determine net force

Both forces act to the right, so we sum:

\[ F_\mathrm{net} = F_{21} + F_{23} = 1.35 + 0.675 = 2.025 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Answer: \[ F_\mathrm{net} \approx 2.025 \, \mathrm{N \, to \, the \, right} \]

Example 3: Three Charges in a Triangle

Problem: Charges \(q_1 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_2 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_3 = -1 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are at the corners of an equilateral triangle of side 0.3 m. Find the net force on \(q_1\).

Visual Representation:

Figure: Forces on \(q_1\) from \(q_2\) and \(q_3\), with x and y components illustrated.

Solution:

Step 1: Calculate forces using Coulomb's Law

\[ F_{12} = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} = (9 \times 10^9) \frac{(2 \times 10^{-6})(3 \times 10^{-6})}{0.3^2} = 0.6 \, \mathrm{N} \] \[ F_{13} = k \frac{|q_1 q_3|}{r^2} = (9 \times 10^9) \frac{(2 \times 10^{-6})(1 \times 10^{-6})}{0.3^2} = 0.2 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Step 2: Resolve each force into components

Assume \(q_1\) at origin (the left), \(q_2\) to the right, \(q_3\) top-vertex (the pointy-end at the top). For equilateral triangle, angle = 60°:

\[ F_{12,x} = F_{12} = 0.6 \, \mathrm{N}, \quad F_{12,y} = 0 \] \[ F_{13,x} = F_{13} \cos 60^\circ = 0.2 \cdot 0.5 = 0.1 \, \mathrm{N}, \quad F_{13,y} = F_{13} \sin 60^\circ = 0.2 \cdot 0.866 \approx 0.173 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Step 3: Sum components

\[ F_x = F_{12,x} + F_{13,x} = 0.6 + 0.1 = 0.7 \, \mathrm{N} \] \[ F_y = F_{12,y} + F_{13,y} = 0 + 0.173 = 0.173 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Step 4: Determine net force and direction

\[ F_\mathrm{net} = \sqrt{F_x^2 + F_y^2} = \sqrt{0.7^2 + 0.173^2} \approx 0.718 \, \mathrm{N} \] \[ \theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{F_y}{F_x}\right) = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{0.173}{0.7}\right) \approx 13.9^\circ \text{ above x-axis} \]

Answer: \[ F_\mathrm{net} \approx 0.718 \, \mathrm{N}, \quad \theta \approx 13.9^\circ \text{ above the horizontal to the right} \]

Example 4: Mixed Charges in a Line

Problem: Charges \(q_1 = 1 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_2 = -2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_3 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are in a straight line with equal spacing 0.25 m. Find the net force on \(q_1\).

Solution:

Step 1: Identify forces on \(q_1\)

\(q_1\) experiences forces due to \(q_2\) and \(q_3\). Coulomb’s Law:

\[ F = k \frac{|q_1 q|}{r^2}, \quad k = 9 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N\,m^2/C^2} \]

Step 2: Force due to \(q_2\)

Distance \(r = 0.25 \, \mathrm{m}\). Attractive → right:

\[ F_{12} = (9 \times 10^9) \frac{(1 \times 10^{-6})(2 \times 10^{-6})}{0.25^2} = 0.288 \, \mathrm{N \, right} \]

Step 3: Force due to \(q_3\)

Distance \(r = 0.5 \, \mathrm{m}\). Repulsive → left:

\[ F_{13} = (9 \times 10^9) \frac{(1 \times 10^{-6})(3 \times 10^{-6})}{0.5^2} = 0.108 \, \mathrm{N \, left} \]

Step 4: Determine net force

Rightward positive, leftward negative:

\[ F_\mathrm{net} = F_{12} - F_{13} = 0.288 - 0.108 = 0.18 \, \mathrm{N \, right} \]

Answer: \[ F_\mathrm{net} \approx 0.18 \, \mathrm{N \, to \, the right} \]

Key Points

  • The net force on a charge is the vector sum of the forces due to all other charges.
  • Each pair of charges interacts according to Coulomb’s law.
  • Pay attention to direction: like charges repel, unlike charges attract.
  • For charges not in a line, resolve forces into components to sum correctly.

Coulomb's Law Practice Questions (Three Charges)

Use the formula \[ F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} \] unless otherwise stated. Take \(k = 9 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N\,m^2/C^2}\). Consider directions (attraction/repulsion) carefully.

  1. Three charges \(q_1 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_2 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_3 = -1 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are in a straight line, 0.2 m apart. Find the net force on \(q_1\).
  2. Using the same charges and positions as Question 1, find the net force on \(q_2\).
  3. Three charges \(q_1 = 1 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_2 = -2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_3 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are in a straight line, 0.25 m apart. Find the net force on \(q_3\).
  4. Charges \(q_1 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_2 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_3 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are at the corners of an equilateral triangle, side 0.3 m. Find the net force on \(q_1\).
  5. Three charges \(q_1 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_2 = -1 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_3 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) in a line, 0.4 m apart. If the distance between \(q_1\) and \(q_3\) is halved, calculate the new net force on \(q_2\).
  6. Charges \(q_1 = -1 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_2 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_3 = -3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are in a straight line, equal spacing 0.2 m. If \(q_1\) is doubled, find the new net force on \(q_3\).
