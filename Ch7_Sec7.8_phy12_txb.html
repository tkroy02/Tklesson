Section 7.8: Problem-Solving Strategies

Quantum physics problems often involve energy levels, wavefunctions, and probability amplitudes. Developing systematic strategies helps solve both conceptual and calculation problems effectively.

Strategies:
  • Identify known and unknown quantities: \( E_n, \psi(x), V(x), L, n \).
  • Choose the appropriate model: particle in a box, harmonic oscillator, potential step, etc.
  • Check boundary conditions for wavefunctions.
  • Use normalization for probability amplitudes.
  • Apply relevant formulas: \( E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2} \) for infinite well, \( \psi_n(x) \) forms, or Schr√∂dinger equation.
  • For energy transitions, apply \( \Delta E = E_{final} - E_{initial} \).
  • Interpret physical meaning after calculations.

Example 1

Determine the energy difference between the first and second levels of a 1D infinite potential well of length 1 nm.

\( E_n = \frac{n^2 h^2}{8 m L^2} \)
\( E_1 = \frac{1^2 h^2}{8 m L^2} \), \( E_2 = \frac{4 h^2}{8 m L^2} \)
\( \Delta E = E_2 - E_1 = \frac{3 h^2}{8 m L^2} \)

Practice Problems

  1. Normalize the wavefunction \( \psi(x) = A \sin(\pi x / L) \) for a 1D box.
  2. Find the energy of the 3rd level for a particle in a 2 nm box.
  3. Sketch wavefunctions for \( n = 1,2,3 \) and indicate nodes.
  4. Calculate the wavelength of light emitted when particle transitions from level 2 to 1 in a 1 nm well.
  5. Given a finite potential well, discuss qualitatively how the energy levels differ from an infinite well.
  6. Explain why quantum numbers are integers in bound systems.
  7. Compute \( \Delta E \) for a particle transitioning from n=4 to n=2 in a 3 nm well.
  8. Discuss physical interpretation of the probability density \( |\psi(x)|^2 \).
  9. Apply strategies to solve a particle encountering a potential step.
  10. Summarize systematic steps to approach any 1D quantum mechanics problem.
