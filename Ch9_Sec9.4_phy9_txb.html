Section 9.4: Types of Reflection of Light

Reflection of light can occur in two main forms: Specular (Regular) Reflection and Diffuse Reflection. Understanding these types is crucial for optics and daily-life applications.

Specular Reflection

Specular reflection occurs when light reflects from a smooth, polished surface such as a plane mirror or calm water. The reflected rays remain parallel, forming a clear and sharp image.

  • Surface: Smooth and shiny.
  • Reflected rays: Parallel.
  • Image: Clear, upright, same size as object.

Figure 9.4.1: Specular reflection of light from a plane mirror.

Diffuse Reflection

Diffuse reflection occurs when light strikes a rough surface such as paper, wall, or unpolished wood. The reflected rays scatter in different directions, preventing a clear image formation.

  • Surface: Rough or uneven.
  • Reflected rays: Scattered.
  • Image: No clear image is formed; only illumination.

Figure 9.4.2: Diffuse reflection from a rough surface.

Example 1: Distinguishing Reflection Types

Problem: Determine whether the following surfaces produce specular or diffuse reflection: calm water, white paper, polished metal.

Solution:

  • Calm water → Specular reflection (smooth surface, parallel rays).
  • White paper → Diffuse reflection (rough surface, scattered rays).
  • Polished metal → Specular reflection (shiny, smooth surface).

Key Points

  • Specular reflection produces clear images; diffuse reflection does not.
  • Both follow the law of reflection locally at each point of incidence.
  • Specular reflection is used in mirrors, telescopes, and optical devices.
  • Diffuse reflection ensures uniform illumination of surfaces in rooms, streets, and displays.
