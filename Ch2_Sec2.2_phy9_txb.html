Section 2.2: SI Base Units

The International System of Units (SI) is the standard system of measurement used globally in science, engineering, and everyday life. It provides a consistent framework that allows measurements to be communicated clearly and accurately across the world.

The SI system is built on seven base quantities. Every other unit in physics, called a derived unit, comes from combining these seven base units logically and mathematically.

The Seven SI Base Units

Each base quantity has a specific unit and symbol, shown in the table below:

Base Quantity SI Unit Symbol
Length meter m
Mass kilogram kg
Time second s
Electric Current ampere A
Thermodynamic Temperature kelvin K
Amount of Substance mole mol
Luminous Intensity candela cd
Note: Derived units, like Newtons for force or Joules for energy, are combinations of these seven base units. Example: Force (Newton, N) is defined as kg·m/s².

Why SI Units Are Important

Before SI units were established, different regions and countries used their own units of measurement. This caused confusion and made collaboration difficult, especially in science and international trade.

  • Scientists can replicate experiments accurately, no matter where they are in the world.
  • Engineers avoid costly mistakes on international projects by using a common standard.
  • Students learn a unified system, making communication easier.

Examples of SI Units in Action

  • Measuring the distance between two cities in kilometers (km).
  • Weighing an object in kilograms (kg).
  • Timing a race in seconds (s).
  • Recording electric current in a circuit in amperes (A).
  • Measuring temperature in kelvins (K) or degrees Celsius (°C, derived from K).
