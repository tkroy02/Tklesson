Section 8.3: Eddy Currents & Applications

Eddy currents are circulating currents induced in a conductor when it experiences a changing magnetic field. They are responsible for energy losses in some devices but are also exploited in applications like magnetic braking and induction heating.

Eddy Currents:

Circulating currents in a conductor caused by changing magnetic flux, often leading to resistive heating.

Energy Loss:

Eddy currents produce resistive losses given by \( P = I^2 R \).

Applications:
  • Magnetic braking in trains and roller coasters
  • Induction heating for cooking and metal processing
  • Metal detectors
  • Reducing flux in transformers using laminated cores

Example 1

A copper plate of resistance 0.1 Ω experiences a changing magnetic flux inducing a current of 2 A. Calculate the power loss due to eddy currents.

Power loss: \( P = I^2 R = (2)^2 \times 0.1 = 0.4 \text{ W} \)

Practice Problems

  1. A solid metal plate moves into a region of changing magnetic field. Explain why eddy currents are stronger in a thick plate than a thin one.
  2. Calculate the eddy current power loss if a 5-turn copper disc of resistance 0.05 Ω has a current of 3 A induced.
  3. Explain how lamination of transformer cores reduces eddy current losses.
  4. Design an experiment to demonstrate magnetic braking using a non-magnetic aluminum plate and a magnet.
  5. Explain why induction cookers heat the metal pot but not the ceramic base.
