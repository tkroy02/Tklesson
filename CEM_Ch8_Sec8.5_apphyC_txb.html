Section 8.5: Mutual Induction

Mutual induction occurs when a changing current in one coil induces an EMF in a nearby coil. The proportionality between the EMF induced in the second coil and the rate of change of current in the first coil is defined by the mutual inductance.

Mutual Inductance:

\[ \mathcal{E}_2 = -M \frac{dI_1}{dt} \]

Where \( \mathcal{E}_2 \) is the EMF induced in coil 2, \( M \) is the mutual inductance, and \( \frac{dI_1}{dt} \) is the rate of change of current in coil 1.

Unit of Mutual Inductance:

1 Henry (H) is the mutual inductance if a rate of change of 1 A/s in coil 1 induces 1 V in coil 2.

Applications:
  • Transformers for voltage step-up or step-down
  • Inductive coupling in circuits
  • Wireless charging systems

Example 1

A primary coil of 100 turns carries a current increasing at 5 A/s. If a secondary coil of 50 turns is nearby and the mutual inductance is 0.02 H, calculate the EMF induced in the secondary coil.

Induced EMF: \( \mathcal{E}_2 = -M \frac{dI_1}{dt} = -0.02 \times 5 = -0.1 \, \text{V} \)

Practice Problems

  1. Two coils have mutual inductance 0.05 H. If the current in the primary changes at 3 A/s, calculate the induced EMF in the secondary coil.
  2. Explain why transformers rely on mutual induction.
  3. A coil induces 0.2 V in a nearby coil when its current changes at 10 A/s. Determine the mutual inductance.
  4. Describe an application of mutual induction in wireless charging devices.
  5. If the mutual inductance between two coils is doubled, what happens to the induced EMF for the same rate of current change?
