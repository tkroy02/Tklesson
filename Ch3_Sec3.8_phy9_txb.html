3.8 Motion with Constant Acceleration

When an object’s acceleration is constant, its motion becomes predictable, and we can use specific equations to describe its behavior. These equations are known as the Equations of Motion.

Key Concept: Constant acceleration means that velocity changes by equal amounts in equal intervals of time.

First Equation of Motion

The first equation relates initial velocity, final velocity, acceleration, and time:

\[ v = u + at \]

Where: \( v \) = final velocity, \( u \) = initial velocity, \( a \) = constant acceleration, \( t \) = time.

Derivation: Acceleration is defined as the rate of change of velocity: \[ a = \frac{v - u}{t} \] Rearranging gives: \[ v = u + at \]

Second Equation of Motion

The second equation connects displacement to velocity, acceleration, and time:

\[ s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \]

Where: \( s \) = displacement.

Derivation: Displacement is the area under a velocity-time graph. For constant acceleration, the graph is a trapezium. \[ s = \text{average velocity} \times t \] The average velocity is \( \frac{u + v}{2} \). Substituting \( v = u + at \): \[ s = \frac{u + (u + at)}{2} \times t \] Simplifying: \[ s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \]

Third Equation of Motion

The third equation relates velocity and displacement directly, without time:

\[ v^2 = u^2 + 2as \]

Derivation: From the first equation: \( t = \frac{v - u}{a} \). Substituting into the second equation: \[ s = u\left(\frac{v - u}{a}\right) + \frac{1}{2}a\left(\frac{v - u}{a}\right)^2 \] Simplifying and rearranging: \[ v^2 = u^2 + 2as \]

Summary of Equations

Equation Relationship Used When
1st \( v = u + at \) Finding velocity after time with constant acceleration
2nd \( s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \) Finding displacement after a given time
3rd \( v^2 = u^2 + 2as \) Finding velocity or displacement without involving time

Real-Life Example

When a car accelerates from rest to highway speed, engineers use these equations to predict how far it will travel and how long it will take. Similarly, in sports, these equations help determine how quickly a sprinter reaches top speed.

Worked Example 1: Car Accelerating from Rest

A car starts from rest and accelerates uniformly at \( 2 \, m/s^2 \) for \( 6 \, s \). Calculate:

  1. Its velocity after 6 seconds
  2. The distance traveled in that time

Solution:

Given:

  • Initial velocity, \( u = 0 \, m/s \)
  • Acceleration, \( a = 2 \, m/s^2 \)
  • Time, \( t = 6 \, s \)

Step 1: Velocity after 6 seconds

Using the first equation of motion:

\[ v = u + at \]

Substituting values: \[ v = 0 + (2)(6) = 12 \, m/s \]

Final velocity, \( v = 12 \, m/s \)

Step 2: Distance traveled in 6 seconds

Using the second equation of motion:

\[ s = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2 \]

Substituting values: \[ s = (0)(6) + \frac{1}{2}(2)(6)^2 \] \[ s = 1 \times 36 = 36 \, m \]

Distance traveled, \( s = 36 \, m \)

Worked Example 2: Train Slowing Down

A train moving at \( 25 \, m/s \) comes to a stop after traveling \( 100 \, m \) with uniform deceleration. Find its deceleration.

Solution:

Given:

  • Initial velocity, \( u = 25 \, m/s \)
  • Final velocity, \( v = 0 \, m/s \)
  • Displacement, \( s = 100 \, m \)

Using the third equation of motion:

\[ v^2 = u^2 + 2as \]

Rearranging to solve for \( a \):

\[ a = \frac{v^2 - u^2}{2s} \]

Substituting values: \[ a = \frac{(0)^2 - (25)^2}{2(100)} \] \[ a = \frac{-625}{200} = -3.125 \, m/s^2 \]

Deceleration, \( a = -3.13 \, m/s^2 \)
(negative sign indicates slowing down)

Practice Problems: Motion with Constant Acceleration

Solve these to apply the equations of motion:

  1. A car starts from rest and accelerates at \(2 \, m/s^2\). What is its velocity after 8 seconds?
  2. A bike moving at 10 m/s comes to a stop in 5 seconds with uniform deceleration. Find its deceleration.
  3. An object starts at 5 m/s and accelerates uniformly at 1.5 m/s² for 6 seconds. Calculate the distance covered.
  4. A car travels 100 m while accelerating from 20 m/s to 30 m/s. Find its acceleration.
  5. A train accelerates from rest to 25 m/s in 50 s. Determine its acceleration and distance covered.
  6. A rock is dropped from a cliff (g = 9.8 m/s²). Calculate the distance it falls in 4 seconds.
