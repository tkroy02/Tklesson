Section 1.5: Significant Figures

Significant figures (often abbreviated as "sig figs") indicate the precision of a measured or calculated quantity. They help convey how accurate a measurement is and ensure consistency in calculations.

Key Concept: Only digits that carry meaning contribute to significant figures. Leading zeros are never significant, trailing zeros may or may not be significant depending on context.

Rules for Counting Significant Figures

  • All non-zero digits are significant. Example: 123 → 3 sig figs
  • Zeros between non-zero digits are significant. Example: 1002 → 4 sig figs
  • Leading zeros are NOT significant. Example: 0.005 → 1 sig fig
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal point are significant. Example: 2.500 → 4 sig figs
  • Trailing zeros without a decimal point are ambiguous. Example: 2500 → 2–4 sig figs (use scientific notation to clarify)

Operations with Significant Figures

  • Addition/Subtraction: Result should have the same number of decimal places as the measurement with the fewest decimal places.

    Example: 12.11 + 0.3 = 12.41 → 12.4

  • Multiplication/Division: Result should have the same number of significant figures as the measurement with the fewest sig figs.

    Example: 4.56 × 1.4 = 6.384 → 6.4

Examples in Physics

  • Mass of an electron: \(9.11 \times 10^{-31}\) kg → 3 sig figs
  • Distance to moon: 384,400 km → 6 sig figs
  • Time measured with a stopwatch: 12.5 s → 3 sig figs

Review Questions

  1. How many significant figures are in 0.00720?
  2. Round 45.678 to 3 significant figures.
  3. Calculate 12.11 + 0.3 and report the result with correct sig figs.
  4. Multiply 6.2 × 3.45 and give the answer with the correct number of sig figs.
  5. Explain why significant figures are important in scientific measurements.

Key Takeaways

  • Significant figures indicate the precision of a number or measurement.
  • Follow specific rules to determine how many digits are significant.
  • Always use proper sig figs in calculations to maintain accuracy and consistency.
