4.2 Newton's First Law of Motion

Newton's First Law of Motion is also called the law of inertia. It describes how objects behave when there is no net force acting on them.

Definition: An object remains at rest, or continues moving in a straight line at constant speed, unless acted upon by a net external force.

Understanding the Law

This law tells us that motion does not change without a cause. A moving object will not stop or change direction on its own. A stationary object will not start moving unless a net force acts on it.

Everyday life seems to contradict this because friction and air resistance provide unbalanced forces that slow objects down. For example, when you slide a book across a table, it stops because of friction, not because the book "runs out of motion."

Inertia

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. The greater the mass of an object, the greater its inertia.

  • A heavy truck is harder to start or stop than a small car because it has more mass and more inertia.
  • Seatbelts protect passengers during sudden stops by providing the unbalanced force needed to stop their motion.
Summary: Mass is a measure of inertia. More mass → more inertia.

Examples of Newton's First Law

  • A soccer ball will stay at rest on the field until a player kicks it.
  • A moving car continues to roll even after the engine is turned off, but slows down due to friction and air resistance.
  • A coin on a card falls straight down into a glass when the card is quickly flicked away—the coin was at rest and resisted change.

Illustration: Coin and Card Trick

When a card is flicked out from under a coin, the coin drops straight down into a glass. This demonstrates inertia: the coin resists horizontal motion because no net force acts horizontally.
