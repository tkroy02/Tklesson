Section 6.1: Kirchhoff’s Laws

Kirchhoff’s Laws are fundamental for analyzing complex circuits that cannot be simplified using simple series and parallel combinations.

Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL):

The sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum of currents leaving the junction. Mathematically:

\[ \sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out} \]

Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL):

The sum of potential differences around any closed loop is zero:

\[ \sum V = 0 \]

Example 1

Find the currents in a simple two-loop circuit using Kirchhoff’s Laws.

Define loop currents I₁ and I₂. Apply KVL to each loop:

\[ \text{Loop 1: } 10 - 2I_1 - 3(I_1 - I_2) = 0 \]

\[ \text{Loop 2: } 5 - 5I_2 - 3(I_2 - I_1) = 0 \]

Solve the simultaneous equations to find I₁ = 2 A, I₂ = 1 A.

Practice Problems

  1. Apply KCL at a junction where three currents meet: 2 A, 3 A entering. What is the outgoing current?
  2. For a single loop with a 12 V battery and resistors 2 Ω, 3 Ω, and 5 Ω, calculate the loop current using KVL.
  3. Two-loop circuit: Assign currents and apply KVL to solve for unknowns.
  4. Explain the physical significance of KCL and KVL in simple words.
  5. Design a simple three-loop circuit and calculate all branch currents using Kirchhoff’s Laws.
