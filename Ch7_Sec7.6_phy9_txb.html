Linear Expansion

Linear expansion occurs along one dimension. The change in length of a rod or wire due to temperature is proportional to the initial length and temperature change:

\( \Delta L = \alpha L_0 \Delta T \)

  • \( \Delta L \) = change in length (m)
  • \( \alpha \) = coefficient of linear expansion (1/K)
  • \( L_0 \) = initial length (m)
  • \( \Delta T = T_\text{final} - T_\text{initial} \) (K or °C)
Example: A 2 m steel rod (\( \alpha = 1.2 \times 10^{-5} \, K^{-1}\)) is heated by 50 K.
Solution: \( \Delta L = 1.2 \times 10^{-5} \times 2 \times 50 = 0.0012 \, \text{m} = 1.2 \, \text{mm} \)

Area Expansion

When a surface is heated, its area expands. The change in area is proportional to the initial area and temperature change:

\( \Delta A = \beta A_0 \Delta T \)

  • \( \Delta A \) = change in area (m²)
  • \( A_0 \) = initial area (m²)
  • \( \beta \) = coefficient of area expansion (≈ 2α for solids) (1/K)
Example: A 2 m² metal plate (\( \beta = 2.4 \times 10^{-5} \, K^{-1}\)) is heated by 100 K.
Solution: \( \Delta A = 2.4 \times 10^{-5} \times 2 \times 100 = 0.0048 \, \text{m²} \)

Volume Expansion

The volume of solids and liquids increases with temperature. The change in volume is proportional to the initial volume and temperature change:

\( \Delta V = \gamma V_0 \Delta T \)

  • \( \Delta V \) = change in volume (m³)
  • \( V_0 \) = initial volume (m³)
  • \( \gamma \) = coefficient of volume expansion (≈ 3α for solids) (1/K)
Example: A 1 m³ tank of water (\( \gamma = 3.0 \times 10^{-4} \, K^{-1}\)) is heated by 40 K.
Solution: \( \Delta V = 3.0 \times 10^{-4} \times 1 \times 40 = 0.012 \, \text{m³} \)

Applications of Thermal Expansion

  • Bridges and railways include expansion joints to prevent structural damage.
  • Thermometers use liquid expansion to measure temperature changes.
  • Metal lids on glass jars can be loosened by heating due to expansion.
  • Engine components account for expansion to maintain tolerances at high temperatures.
