5.6 Simple Machines

A simple machine is a device that changes the direction or magnitude of a force to make work easier. Simple machines help us apply smaller forces over larger distances to achieve the same work.

Key Idea: Simple machines do not reduce the total work required; they trade off force and distance.

Types of Simple Machines

  • Lever: A rigid bar that pivots around a fulcrum. Example: seesaw, crowbar.
  • Inclined Plane: A sloped surface that reduces the force needed to raise objects. Example: ramp.
  • Pulley: A wheel with a rope that changes the direction of force. Example: flagpole.
  • Wheel and Axle: Reduces friction to move objects. Example: rolling pin, doorknob.
  • Screw: An inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder. Example: jar lid, screw fastener.
  • Wedge: A double inclined plane that splits or lifts objects. Example: knife, axe.

Mechanical Advantage (MA)

Mechanical advantage tells us how much a simple machine multiplies the input force:

\[ MA = \frac{F_\text{output}}{F_\text{input}} \]

- If \( MA > 1 \), the machine reduces the effort force.
- If \( MA < 1 \), the machine increases speed or distance but requires more force.

Note: Ideal mechanical advantage ignores friction.

Simple Machines and Work

While simple machines make it easier to apply a force, the total work done remains the same. Work is defined as:

\[ W = F \cdot d \]

If a machine reduces the force, the distance over which the force is applied increases proportionally, keeping the total work constant.

Levers trade off force and distance to do the same work.

Practice Problems

  1. A lever allows a person to lift a 100 N load using 25 N of effort. Calculate the mechanical advantage.
  2. An inclined plane is 5 m long and raises a box to a height of 1 m. Determine the ideal mechanical advantage.
  3. A pulley system lets a worker lift a 200 kg load with a 50 kg input force. Calculate the mechanical advantage.
