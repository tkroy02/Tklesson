Section 7.8: Heat Engines

A heat engine is a device that converts thermal energy into mechanical work by transferring heat from a high-temperature reservoir to a low-temperature reservoir. Heat engines are the foundation of most modern power plants and engines.

Basic Concept of Heat Engines

Heat engines operate in cycles. Heat \(Q_H\) is absorbed from the hot reservoir, part of it is converted into work \(W\), and the remaining heat \(Q_C\) is expelled to the cold reservoir. The performance of a heat engine is measured by its efficiency.

\( \eta = \frac{W}{Q_H} = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H} \)

  • \( \eta \) = efficiency of the engine (fraction or %)
  • \( W \) = work done (J)
  • \( Q_H \) = heat absorbed from hot reservoir (J)
  • \( Q_C \) = heat rejected to cold reservoir (J)

Examples of Heat Engines

  • Steam engines in trains and power plants
  • Internal combustion engines in cars and motorcycles
  • Jet engines in airplanes
  • Stirling engines in some renewable energy systems
  • Gas turbines in electricity generation

Carnot Engine

The Carnot engine is an idealized engine with maximum efficiency. Its efficiency depends only on the temperatures of the hot and cold reservoirs:

\( \eta_\text{Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H} \)

  • \( T_H \) = temperature of hot reservoir (K)
  • \( T_C \) = temperature of cold reservoir (K)
Example: If a heat engine operates between 500 K and 300 K, its maximum efficiency is:
\( \eta_\text{max} = 1 - \frac{300}{500} = 0.4 \) or 40%.

Real vs Ideal Heat Engines

  • Real engines have friction and other losses, so their efficiency is always less than the Carnot limit.
  • Ideal engines like Carnot assume no energy losses and reversible processes.
  • Understanding ideal engines helps engineers design more efficient practical engines.
