Section 10.3: Coulomb's Law

Coulomb's Law describes the electrostatic force between two point charges. The magnitude of the force is directly proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Mathematically:

\[ F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} \]
  • \(F\) = magnitude of electrostatic force (N)
  • \(q_1, q_2\) = magnitudes of the two charges (C)
  • \(r\) = distance between the charges (m)
  • \(k = 8.99 \times 10^9 \, \mathrm{N \cdot m^2 / C^2}\) (Coulomb's constant)

The direction of the force depends on the type of charges: like charges repel, and unlike charges attract.

Example 1: Like Charges

Problem: Two protons, each with charge \(q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \mathrm{C}\), are 0.05 m apart. Find the electrostatic force between them.

Solution:

\[ F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} = 8.99 \times 10^9 \frac{(1.6 \times 10^{-19})^2}{0.05^2} \] \[ F \approx 9.18 \times 10^{-26} \, \mathrm{N \quad (repulsive)} \]

Example 2: Unlike Charges

Problem: An electron (\(q = -1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \mathrm{C}\)) is 0.02 m away from a proton (\(q = +1.6 \times 10^{-19} \, \mathrm{C}\)). Find the force.

Solution:

\[ F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} = 8.99 \times 10^9 \frac{(1.6 \times 10^{-19})^2}{0.02^2} \] \[ F \approx 5.75 \times 10^{-25} \, \mathrm{N \quad (attractive)} \]

Example 3: Effect of Distance Halved

Problem: Two charges of \(4 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) and \(6 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are 0.1 m apart. If the distance is halved, what happens to the force?

Solution:

\[ F_1 = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} = 8.99 \times 10^9 \frac{(4 \times 10^{-6})(6 \times 10^{-6})}{0.1^2} = 21.6 \, \mathrm{N} \] \[ r_{\text{new}} = 0.05 \, \mathrm{m} \quad \Rightarrow \quad F_2 = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r_{\text{new}}^2} = 8.99 \times 10^9 \frac{24 \times 10^{-12}}{0.0025} \] \[ F_2 \approx 86.4 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Observation: Halving the distance quadruples the force, consistent with the inverse-square law.

Example 4: Effect of Doubling Charges

Problem: Two charges \(q_1 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), \(q_2 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are 0.1 m apart. If both charges are doubled, find the new force.

Solution:

\[ F_1 = k \frac{2 \times 10^{-6} \cdot 3 \times 10^{-6}}{0.1^2} = 5.4 \, \mathrm{N} \] \[ q_1' = 4 \, \mu\mathrm{C}, \, q_2' = 6 \, \mu\mathrm{C} \quad \Rightarrow \quad F_2 = k \frac{4 \times 10^{-6} \cdot 6 \times 10^{-6}}{0.1^2} = 21.6 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Observation: Doubling both charges quadruples the force.

Example 5: Combined Effect

Problem: Two charges \(q_1 = 1 \, \mu\mathrm{C}, q_2 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are 0.2 m apart. If the distance is halved and the charges are doubled, what is the new force?

Solution:

\[ F_1 = k \frac{1 \times 10^{-6} \cdot 2 \times 10^{-6}}{0.2^2} = 0.4495 \, \mathrm{N} \] \[ q_1' = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}, q_2' = 4 \, \mu\mathrm{C}, r' = 0.1 \, \mathrm{m} \] \[ F_2 = k \frac{2 \times 10^{-6} \cdot 4 \times 10^{-6}}{0.1^2} \approx 7.192 \, \mathrm{N} \]

Observation: Doubling the charges and halving the distance increases the force by more than 16 times.

Key Points

  • Coulombâ€™s Law quantifies the electrostatic force between two point charges.
  • Force is directly proportional to the product of the charges.
  • Force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between charges.
  • Like charges repel; unlike charges attract.
  • Changes in distance or charge magnitude affect the force according to the formula.

Coulomb's Law Practice Questions

Use the formula \( F = k \frac{|q_1 q_2|}{r^2} \) for all questions unless otherwise stated. These questions focus on calculating force, distance, charges, and exploring how changes in distance or charge affect the electrostatic force.

  1. Two charges \(q_1 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) and \(q_2 = 4 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are 0.2 m apart. Find the electrostatic force between them.
  2. Two charges of \(5 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) and \(2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) experience a force of \(45 \, \mathrm{N}\). Calculate the distance between them.
  3. Two charges are 0.1 m apart and the electrostatic force is \(36 \, \mathrm{N}\). If one charge is \(q_1 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\), find the other charge \(q_2\).
  4. Two charges \(q_1 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}, q_2 = 3 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are 0.3 m apart. If the distance is halved, how does the force change? Calculate the new force.
  5. Two charges \(q_1 = 2 \, \mu\mathrm{C}, q_2 = 4 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are 0.2 m apart. If both charges are doubled, calculate the new force.
  6. Two charges \(q_1 = 1 \, \mu\mathrm{C}, q_2 = 5 \, \mu\mathrm{C}\) are 0.5 m apart. If the distance is reduced to 0.25 m and \(q_1\) is tripled, what is the new force?
