Section 1.2: The Scientific Method

The scientific method is a systematic process used by scientists to investigate questions, test hypotheses, and develop reliable knowledge about the natural world.

Key Concept: The scientific method ensures that results are objective, repeatable, and based on evidence.

The steps of the scientific method typically include:

  • Observation: Gathering data and noticing phenomena.
  • Question: Identifying a specific question or problem to investigate.
  • Hypothesis: Formulating a testable explanation or prediction.
  • Experiment: Designing and conducting experiments to test the hypothesis.
  • Data Analysis: Evaluating results using graphs, statistics, or calculations.
  • Conclusion: Determining whether the hypothesis is supported or refuted.
  • Communication: Sharing findings with the scientific community for review and replication.

Example: Testing Plant Growth

Suppose a scientist wants to investigate whether sunlight affects plant growth. The experiment might look like this:

  • Observation: Some plants grow faster than others.
  • Question: Does sunlight increase the growth rate of plants?
  • Hypothesis: Plants exposed to more sunlight will grow faster.
  • Experiment: Place identical plants in different sunlight conditions and measure growth over two weeks.
  • Data Analysis: Compare average growth between plants in full sun, partial sun, and shade.
  • Conclusion: Determine whether sunlight significantly affected growth.

Theory and Law

In science, theories and laws are both important, but they are different:

  • Theory: A well-substantiated explanation of some aspect of the natural world, based on evidence and repeatedly tested. Example: The theory of evolution explains how species change over time.
  • Law: A statement that describes a consistent natural phenomenon, often expressed mathematically, but does not explain why it occurs. Example: Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation describes how objects attract each other with a force proportional to their masses.
Remember: Laws describe patterns; theories explain them.

Relationship Between Theories and Laws

A common misconception is that theories eventually “become” laws when proven. This is incorrect. Theories and laws serve different purposes.

  • Theories explain why phenomena occur.
  • Laws describe what happens, usually with concise statements or formulas.

In fact, many scientific ideas are both theories and laws at the same time:

  • Example: Newton’s Law of Gravitation describes the force between masses, while Einstein’s Theory of General Relativity explains why gravity occurs as a result of spacetime curvature.
  • The Kinetic Molecular Theory explains the behavior of gases, while the Ideal Gas Law mathematically describes how pressure, volume, and temperature are related.
Key Point: Theories do not turn into laws. Instead, they work together: laws tell us what happens, and theories tell us why.

Key Takeaways

  • The scientific method is a structured approach to acquiring reliable knowledge.
  • Theories explain phenomena, while laws describe consistent patterns.
  • Theories do not become laws — both work together to build scientific understanding.
  • Sharing results and replication ensure that science remains accurate and reliable.
