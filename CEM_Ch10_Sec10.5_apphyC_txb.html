Section 10.5: Maxwell’s Equations in Free Space

Maxwell’s equations in free space summarize the behavior of electric and magnetic fields in regions with no charges or currents.

1. Gauss’s Law for Electricity: \[ \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = 0 \]
2. Gauss’s Law for Magnetism: \[ \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0 \]
3. Faraday’s Law: \[ \nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \]
4. Ampère-Maxwell Law: \[ \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} \]

Example

Show that an electromagnetic wave satisfies the wave equation in free space.

Take curl of Faraday's Law and substitute Ampère-Maxwell Law:

\[ \nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{E}) = -\frac{\partial}{\partial t} (\nabla \times \vec{B}) = -\mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} \]

Use vector identity \(\nabla \times (\nabla \times \vec{E}) = \nabla(\nabla \cdot \vec{E}) - \nabla^2 \vec{E} = -\nabla^2 \vec{E}\)

\[ \nabla^2 \vec{E} = \mu_0 \epsilon_0 \frac{\partial^2 \vec{E}}{\partial t^2} \]

Thus, \(\vec{E}\) satisfies the wave equation with speed \( c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu_0 \epsilon_0}} \).
