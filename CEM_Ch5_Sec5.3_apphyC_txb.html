Section 5.3: Resistivity & Temperature

The resistivity of a material depends on temperature. For most metals, it increases linearly with temperature:

\[ \rho(T) = \rho_0 \left[ 1 + \alpha (T - T_0) \right] \]

where:

  • \(\rho_0\) = resistivity at reference temperature \(T_0\)
  • \(\alpha\) = temperature coefficient of resistivity
  • \(T\) = actual temperature
Key Points:
  • Conductors: resistivity increases with temperature (\(\alpha > 0\)).
  • Semiconductors: resistivity decreases with temperature (\(\alpha < 0\)).
  • Resistivity relates to resistance \( R = \rho \frac{L}{A} \).

Example 1

A copper wire has \(\rho_0 = 1.68 \times 10^{-8}\, \Omega \cdot m\) at 20°C. Find its resistivity at 100°C (\(\alpha = 0.0039 \, \text{°C}^{-1}\)).

\[ \rho = \rho_0 [1 + \alpha (T - T_0)] = 1.68 \times 10^{-8} [1 + 0.0039 (100-20)] \]

\[ \rho \approx 2.21 \times 10^{-8} \, \Omega \cdot m \]

Practice Problems

  1. Define the temperature coefficient of resistivity and give typical values for copper and aluminum.
  2. Calculate the resistance change of a 2 m long aluminum wire when heated from 20°C to 80°C.
  3. Explain why resistivity of semiconductors decreases with temperature.
  4. A material has \(\rho_0 = 5 \times 10^{-7} \, \Omega\cdot m\) at 25°C. Compute \(\rho\) at 75°C if \(\alpha = 0.002\).
  5. Discuss the limitations of linear approximation for resistivity vs temperature.
