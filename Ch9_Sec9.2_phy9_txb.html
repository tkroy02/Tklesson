Section 9.2: Electromagnetic Spectrum

The electromagnetic spectrum is the range of all possible frequencies of electromagnetic radiation. It includes radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays.

Properties of Electromagnetic Waves

  • All electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light in vacuum: \(c = 3.0 \times 10^8 \, \mathrm{m/s}\).
  • They are transverse waves, consisting of oscillating electric and magnetic fields perpendicular to each other and the direction of propagation.
  • They do not require a medium to propagate.
  • Energy of an electromagnetic wave is related to its frequency: \(E = h f\).

Electromagnetic Spectrum Overview

The spectrum is usually presented in order of increasing frequency (or decreasing wavelength):

  • Radio Waves: Longest wavelength, used in communication.
  • Microwaves: Used in cooking and radar.
  • Infrared (IR): Experienced as heat, used in remote controls.

  • Ultraviolet (UV): Causes sunburn, used in sterilization.
  • X-Rays: Used in medical imaging.
  • Gamma Rays: Highest energy, used in cancer treatment and emitted by radioactive substances.

Key Points

  • All electromagnetic waves travel at the same speed in vacuum.
  • Energy of a wave increases with frequency.
  • Wavelength decreases as frequency increases.
  • Different regions of the spectrum have different applications in technology and medicine.
