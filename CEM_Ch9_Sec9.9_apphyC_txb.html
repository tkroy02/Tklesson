Section 9.9: Power in AC Circuits

AC circuits have power that depends on voltage, current, and phase difference. Power can be instantaneous, average, or apparent.

Instantaneous Power:

\[ p(t) = v(t) i(t) = V_m I_m \sin(\omega t) \sin(\omega t - \phi) \]

Average Power:

\[ P_\text{avg} = V_\text{rms} I_\text{rms} \cos \phi \]

\( \cos \phi \) is the **power factor**, indicating how much of the total power is real (useful).

Apparent Power and Reactive Power:

\[ S = V_\text{rms} I_\text{rms}, \quad Q = V_\text{rms} I_\text{rms} \sin \phi \]

\( S \) is apparent power, \( Q \) is reactive power stored in the circuit.

Example 1

An AC circuit has \( V_\text{rms} = 120 \, \text{V} \), \( I_\text{rms} = 10 \, \text{A} \), and phase angle \( \phi = 30^\circ \). Find average, apparent, and reactive power.

Average power: \( P_\text{avg} = V_\text{rms} I_\text{rms} \cos \phi = 120 \cdot 10 \cdot \cos 30^\circ \approx 1039 \, \text{W} \)

Apparent power: \( S = V_\text{rms} I_\text{rms} = 120 \cdot 10 = 1200 \, \text{VA} \)

Reactive power: \( Q = V_\text{rms} I_\text{rms} \sin \phi = 120 \cdot 10 \cdot \sin 30^\circ = 600 \, \text{VAR} \)
