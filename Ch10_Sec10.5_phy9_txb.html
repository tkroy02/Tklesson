Section 10.5: Ohm's Law

This section introduces electric current, voltage (potential difference), and resistance — the three fundamental quantities in simple circuits — and shows how they relate via Ohm's Law.

Electric Current, Voltage, and Resistance

  • Electric current (I): This is the rate of flow of electric charge. The Unit of Electric current is ampere (A). One ampere = one coulomb per second.
  • Voltage / Potential difference (V): This is the energy per unit charge that drives electrons through a circuit. The Unit of Potential difference is volt (V).
  • Resistance (R): This is the opposition to the flow of electric current provided by a material or component. The Unit of Resistance is ohm (Ω).

Intuitively: voltage is the "push", current is the "flow", and resistance is how hard it is for the flow to pass.

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law states that, for many conductors under constant physical conditions, current is directly proportional to the voltage applied across the conductor:

\[ V = I \, R \]

Rearrangements you'll use often:

  • \( I = \dfrac{V}{R} \)
  • \( R = \dfrac{V}{I} \)

Use the triangle: cover the quantity you want to find.

Relationship and intuition

- Doubling the voltage across the same resistor doubles the current (if R constant).
- Doubling the resistance for the same applied voltage halves the current.
- In circuits, wires and components are modelled by resistances; Ohm's law links the measurable voltage and current through each component.

Worked Examples

Example 1 — Finding Current

Problem: A resistor of \(10\,\Omega\) is connected to a \(20\,\text{V}\) battery. Find the current.

Solution:

Given: \(V=20\,\text{V},\; R=10\,\Omega\).

Use \(I=\dfrac{V}{R}\).

\[ I=\frac{20}{10}=2\ \mathrm{A} \]

Answer: \(2\,\mathrm{A}\).

Example 2 — Finding Voltage

Problem: A circuit carries a current \(I=3\,\mathrm{A}\) through a resistor \(R=15\,\Omega\). Find the voltage across the resistor.

Solution:

Given: \(I=3\,\mathrm{A},\; R=15\,\Omega\).

Use \(V=I R\).

\[ V = 3 \times 15 = 45\ \mathrm{V} \]

Answer: \(45\,\mathrm{V}\).

Example 3 — Finding Resistance

Problem: A bulb draws \(0.5\,\mathrm{A}\) when connected to a \(6\,\mathrm{V}\) battery. Find its resistance.

Solution:

Given: \(V=6\,\mathrm{V},\; I=0.5\,\mathrm{A}\).

Use \(R=\dfrac{V}{I}\).

\[ R = \frac{6}{0.5} = 12\ \Omega \]

Answer: \(12\,\Omega\).

Summary

  • \(V\) (volts) is the electrical potential difference.
  • \(I\) (amperes) is the flow of charge.
  • \(R\) (ohms) is the opposition to current.
  • Ohm's law: \(V=IR\). Use the triangle to rearrange quickly.
  • Careful: not every component is ohmic (some are non-linear), but many resistors and conductors at constant temperature obey Ohm's Law.

Practice Questions

  1. A \(12\,\mathrm{V}\) battery is connected across a \(6\,\Omega\) resistor. Find the current.
  2. A current of \(4\,\mathrm{A}\) flows through a resistor of \(8\,\Omega\). What is the voltage across it?
  3. A lamp is connected to a \(24\,\mathrm{V}\) supply and draws \(2\,\mathrm{A}\). Find its resistance.
  4. A heater has a resistance of \(20\,\Omega\). How much current flows if it is connected to \(100\,\mathrm{V}\)?
  5. A current of \(0.25\,\mathrm{A}\) flows through a resistor. If the voltage is \(5\,\mathrm{V}\), find the resistance.
  6. A resistor is connected to a \(30\,\mathrm{V}\) supply and a current of \(0.5\,\mathrm{A}\) flows. What is the resistance?
