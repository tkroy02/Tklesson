Section 6.8: Centrifugal Force

Centrifugal force is a fictitious or pseudo force that appears when analyzing motion from a rotating reference frame. It acts outward, away from the center of rotation, and balances the centripetal force in the rotating frame to make objects appear at rest relative to the rotating frame.

Key Concept: In an inertial frame, only centripetal force exists (toward the center). In a rotating frame, we introduce the centrifugal force pointing outward so that Newton's laws appear to hold.

The Role of Inertia

Centrifugal force is considered a fictitious force because it doesn't exist in an inertial (non-accelerating) reference frame. The feeling of being pushed outward is a result of inertia, not a real force.

Inertia: The natural property of an object to resist any change in its motion. An object in motion continues moving in a straight line unless a net force acts on it.

The Car Analogy

Imagine you are in a car turning left. The car's tires are using friction to pull the car to the left. The car itself (door, seat) pulls your body left—the centripetal force.

The feeling of being pushed to the right is your body's inertia resisting the change in direction. You are not being pushed outward by a new force; you are simply trying to continue moving straight while the car is turning.

In short, from your perspective inside the car, it feels like a centrifugal force is pushing you out. From an outside perspective, your body's inertia is simply continuing straight while a centripetal force changes the car's direction.

Formula for Centrifugal Force

For an object of mass \(m\) rotating with speed \(v\) at a radius \(r\), the centrifugal force is:

\( F_\text{centrifugal} = \frac{m v^2}{r} \)

Its magnitude is equal to the centripetal force but acts outward in the rotating frame.

Example: Passenger in a Rotating Carousel

A 60 kg passenger stands on the edge of a carousel of radius 2 m rotating at 3 m/s. Find the centrifugal force experienced by the passenger in the rotating frame.

Step 1: Identify formula: \( F_\text{centrifugal} = \frac{m v^2}{r} \)

Step 2: Substitute values: \( m = 60 \, \text{kg}, v = 3 \, \text{m/s}, r = 2 \, \text{m} \)

Step 3: Compute: \[ F_\text{centrifugal} = \frac{60 \cdot 3^2}{2} = \frac{60 \cdot 9}{2} = 270 \, \text{N} \]

The passenger experiences a force of 270 N outward from the center in the rotating frame.

Physical Interpretation

  • Centrifugal force is not a “real” force—it arises due to observing motion in a non-inertial (rotating) frame.
  • Its magnitude is equal to the centripetal force required to maintain circular motion.
  • It helps explain why passengers in rotating rides feel pushed outward even though the real force (centripetal) points inward.
  • The outward sensation is due to inertia: the object's tendency to continue in straight-line motion while the rotating frame moves underneath it.
