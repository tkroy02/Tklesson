Section 9.3: Reflection of Light

Reflection of light occurs when a light ray strikes a surface and bounces back into the same medium. It is the reason we can see images in mirrors and shiny surfaces.

Laws of Reflection

  • The angle of incidence (\(i\)) is equal to the angle of reflection (\(r\)): \(i = r\).
  • The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal all lie in the same plane.

Types of Reflection

  • Regular Reflection: Reflection from a smooth surface (e.g., plane mirror). Produces clear images.
  • Diffuse Reflection: Reflection from a rough surface (e.g., wall). Light scatters in many directions; no clear image is formed.

Example 1: Angle of Reflection

Problem: A light ray strikes a plane mirror at an angle of 30° to the normal. Find the angle of reflection.

Given:

  • Angle of incidence: \(i = 30°\)

Solution:

Using the law of reflection:

\[ r = i = 30° \]

Answer: Angle of reflection is \(30°\).

Example 2: Image Formation

Problem: An object is placed 10 cm in front of a plane mirror. Determine the position of the image.

Given:

  • Object distance from mirror: \(u = 10 \, \mathrm{cm}\)

Solution:

For plane mirrors, the image distance equals the object distance behind the mirror:

\[ v = u = 10 \, \mathrm{cm} \]

Answer: Image is 10 cm behind the mirror.

Key Points

  • Reflection is the bouncing back of light into the same medium.
  • The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection.
  • Regular reflection produces clear images; diffuse reflection does not.
  • Plane mirrors always produce virtual, upright, and same-size images.
