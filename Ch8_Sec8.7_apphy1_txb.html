Section 8.7: Viscosity and Laminar Flow

Viscosity is a measure of a fluid’s resistance to flow. Laminar flow occurs when a fluid moves in smooth, parallel layers without mixing, typically at low velocities.

Poiseuille’s Law (for laminar flow in a pipe): \[ Q = \frac{\pi r^4 \Delta P}{8 \eta L} \] Where:
  • \(Q\) = volumetric flow rate
  • \(r\) = pipe radius
  • \(\Delta P\) = pressure difference
  • \(\eta\) = dynamic viscosity
  • \(L\) = length of the pipe
Key Idea: Higher viscosity → slower flow; smaller radius → sharply reduced flow rate.

Example 1

Oil flows through a pipe of radius 0.01 m and length 2 m. Pressure difference 500 Pa, viscosity 0.1 Pa·s. Find flow rate.

\(Q = \pi (0.01)^4 * 500 / (8 * 0.1 * 2) \approx 1.96 \times 10^{-5} \text{ m³/s}\)

Example 2

Water flows through a pipe of radius 0.02 m and length 1 m. Pressure difference 1000 Pa, viscosity 0.001 Pa·s. Find flow rate.

\(Q = \pi (0.02)^4 * 1000 / (8 * 0.001 * 1) \approx 1.26 \times 10^{-3} \text{ m³/s}\)

Practice Problems

  1. Calculate flow rate for water through a 0.015 m radius pipe of length 2 m, ΔP = 800 Pa, η = 0.001 Pa·s.
  2. Oil (η = 0.05 Pa·s) flows through 0.01 m radius pipe of length 1 m with ΔP = 400 Pa. Find flow rate.
  3. Double the pipe radius. How does flow rate change according to Poiseuille’s law?
  4. Pipe length doubles. How does flow rate change?
  5. Find flow rate for glycerin (η = 1.5 Pa·s) through 0.005 m radius, 1 m long pipe, ΔP = 600 Pa.
  6. Explain why laminar flow occurs at low velocities and viscosity’s effect on Reynolds number.
  7. Pipe radius halves, pressure difference unchanged. Compute new flow rate.
  8. Given Q, ΔP, L, and r, determine dynamic viscosity η.
  9. Compare flow rates of two fluids of different viscosities through same pipe.
  10. Determine pressure difference needed to double flow rate in a pipe.
