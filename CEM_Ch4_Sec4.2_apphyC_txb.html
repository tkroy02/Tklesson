Section 4.2: Surface Charge Distribution

In electrostatic equilibrium, charges on a conductor move until they reach a configuration that minimizes repulsive forces. All excess charge resides on the surface of the conductor, and the distribution depends on the surface geometry.

Key Points:
  • Charge accumulates more densely at sharp edges or pointed regions.
  • Surface charge density: \[ \sigma = \frac{dq}{dA} \]
  • Electric field just outside the conductor: \[ E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0} \] directed normal to the surface.

Example 1

A large flat conducting plate carries uniform surface charge density \(\sigma\). Use Gauss’s Law to find the electric field just outside the plate.

Choose a Gaussian "pillbox" surface straddling the plate.

Flux through the pillbox: \[ \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = EA = \frac{Q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0} \]

Enclosed charge: \( Q_{enc} = \sigma A \).

Thus: \[ 2EA = \frac{\sigma A}{\epsilon_0} \quad \Rightarrow \quad E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0} \]

Field points away from the surface if \(\sigma > 0\).

Practice Problems

  1. Explain why charge density is higher at sharp edges of a conductor.
  2. Use Gauss’s Law to determine the field near a spherical conducting shell of radius \(R\) with surface charge density \(\sigma\).
  3. Predict how charge distributes on a conductor with varying curvature.
  4. Why does the electric field inside a conductor vanish despite surface charges?
  5. Conceptual: Explain why lightning is more likely to strike pointed objects like lightning rods.
