Section 8.4: Self-Induction & Inductance

Self-induction occurs when a changing current in a coil induces an EMF in the same coil, opposing the change in current. The property of a coil that quantifies this effect is called inductance.

Self-Induced EMF:

\[ \mathcal{E}_L = -L \frac{dI}{dt} \]

Where \(L\) is the self-inductance and \(dI/dt\) is the rate of change of current.

Inductance of a Solenoid:

\[ L = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{l} \]

Where \(N\) is the number of turns, \(A\) the cross-sectional area, and \(l\) the length of the solenoid.

Energy Stored in an Inductor:

\[ U = \frac{1}{2} L I^2 \]

The magnetic energy is stored in the field of the inductor.

Example 1

A solenoid with 200 turns, length 0.5 m, and cross-sectional area 0.01 m² carries a current increasing at 2 A/s. Find the self-induced EMF. (Take \(\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\) H/m)

Inductance: \( L = \frac{\mu_0 N^2 A}{l} = \frac{4\pi \times 10^{-7} \cdot 200^2 \cdot 0.01}{0.5} \approx 0.0101 \, \text{H} \)

Self-induced EMF: \( \mathcal{E}_L = -L \frac{dI}{dt} = -0.0101 \times 2 \approx -0.0202 \, \text{V} \)

Practice Problems

  1. Calculate the self-induced EMF of a 50-turn coil of inductance 0.1 H when the current increases at 0.5 A/s.
  2. A solenoid has 500 turns, area 0.02 m², and length 0.5 m. Determine its inductance.
  3. Compute the energy stored in an inductor of 0.2 H carrying 3 A.
  4. Explain how self-induction opposes a sudden change in current in a circuit.
  5. A coil of inductance 0.05 H experiences a decreasing current of 4 A/s. Find the magnitude of the induced EMF.
