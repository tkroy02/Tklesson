Section 8.5: The Doppler Effect

The Doppler Effect describes the change in frequency or wavelength of a wave as observed when the source and observer are moving relative to each other.

Concept

When the source of a wave moves toward the observer, the observed frequency increases. When it moves away, the observed frequency decreases. This effect occurs for sound, light, and other waves.

Formula for sound waves:

  • Source and observer moving: \[ f' = f \frac{v \pm v_o}{v \mp v_s} \]
  • \(f'\) = observed frequency
  • \(f\) = source frequency
  • \(v\) = speed of sound in medium
  • \(v_o\) = speed of observer
  • \(v_s\) = speed of source
  • Use \(+\) if motion is toward, \(−\) if motion is away.

Understanding the Signs in the Doppler Formula

The observed frequency depends on the relative motion direction. The convention is:

  • If the observer moves toward the source, add their speed: \(v + v_o\).
  • If the observer moves away from the source, subtract their speed: \(v - v_o\).
  • If the source moves toward the observer, subtract its speed: \(v - v_s\).
  • If the source moves away from the observer, add its speed: \(v + v_s\).

Intuition: Moving toward the wavefronts makes them "hit" the observer more frequently → higher frequency. Moving away stretches the wavefronts → lower frequency.

Example 1: Source Approaching Observer

Problem: A stationary observer hears a siren from an ambulance moving toward them at 20 m/s. The siren frequency is 700 Hz. Speed of sound is 340 m/s. Find the observed frequency.

Given:

  • \(v = 340 \, \mathrm{m/s}\)
  • \(f = 700 \, \mathrm{Hz}\)
  • \(v_s = 20 \, \mathrm{m/s}\) (source toward observer)
  • \(v_o = 0\) (observer stationary)

Solution:

Using the Doppler formula for source moving toward a stationary observer:

\[ f' = f \frac{v + v_o}{v - v_s} \]

Substitute the known values:

\[ f' = 700 \times \frac{340 + 0}{340 - 20} = 700 \times \frac{340}{320} \]

Answer: \( f' \approx 744 \, \mathrm{Hz} \)

Example 2: Source Receding from Observer

Problem: The same ambulance moves away at 20 m/s. Find the observed frequency.

Given:

  • \(v = 340 \, \mathrm{m/s}\)
  • \(f = 700 \, \mathrm{Hz}\)
  • \(v_s = 20 \, \mathrm{m/s}\) (source moving away)
  • \(v_o = 0\) (observer stationary)

Solution:

Using the Doppler formula for source moving away:

\[ f' = f \frac{v + v_o}{v + v_s} \]

Substitute the known values:

\[ f' = 700 \times \frac{340 + 0}{340 + 20} = 700 \times \frac{340}{360} \]

Answer: \( f' \approx 661 \, \mathrm{Hz} \)

Example 3: Observer Moving Toward Stationary Source

Problem: A car moves toward a stationary horn at 15 m/s. Horn frequency is 500 Hz. Speed of sound is 340 m/s. Find observed frequency.

Given:

  • \(v = 340 \, \mathrm{m/s}\)
  • \(f = 500 \, \mathrm{Hz}\)
  • \(v_o = 15 \, \mathrm{m/s}\) (observer toward source)
  • \(v_s = 0\) (source stationary)

Solution:

Since the observer moves toward the source:

\[ f' = f \frac{v + v_o}{v - v_s} \]

Substitute values:

\[ f' = 500 \times \frac{340 + 15}{340 - 0} = 500 \times \frac{355}{340} \]

Answer: \( f' \approx 522 \, \mathrm{Hz} \)

Example 4: Observer Moving Away from Stationary Source

Problem: A person walks away from a stationary horn at 5 m/s. The horn emits a frequency of 400 Hz. Speed of sound is 340 m/s. Find the observed frequency.

Given:

  • \(v = 340 \, \mathrm{m/s}\)
  • \(f = 400 \, \mathrm{Hz}\)
  • \(v_o = 5 \, \mathrm{m/s}\) (observer moving away from source)
  • \(v_s = 0\) (source stationary)

Solution:

Since the observer is moving away, the observed frequency decreases:

\[ f' = f \frac{v - v_o}{v - v_s} \]

Substitute the known values:

\[ f' = 400 \times \frac{340 - 5}{340 - 0} = 400 \times \frac{335}{340} \]

Answer: \( f' \approx 394 \, \mathrm{Hz} \)

Key Points

  • The Doppler Effect explains frequency changes due to relative motion between source and observer.
  • For sound, the medium affects speed, but not the Doppler formula directly.
  • Positive velocities toward each other increase observed frequency; moving apart decreases it.
  • The same principles apply to light waves, though relativistic effects must be considered at very high speeds.
