Section 1.6: Accuracy and Precision

In measurements and experiments, it is essential to understand both accuracy and precision. They describe how close measurements are to the true value and how consistent repeated measurements are.

Key Concept: A measurement can be precise but not accurate, accurate but not precise, or both.

Definitions

  • Accuracy: How close a measurement is to the true or accepted value.

    Example: If the true length of a table is 2.00 m, a measurement of 2.01 m is accurate.

  • Precision: How close repeated measurements are to each other.

    Example: Repeated measurements of 2.10 m, 2.11 m, 2.09 m are precise, but not accurate if the true value is 2.00 m.

Relationship Between Accuracy and Precision

  • Measurements can be:
    • Accurate and precise (ideal scenario)
    • Accurate but not precise (close to true value, scattered readings)
    • Precise but not accurate (readings are consistent but far from true value)
    • Neither accurate nor precise (random and far from true value)
  • Accuracy ensures correctness, precision ensures consistency.

Examples in Physics

  • Length of a lab bench measured as 1.98 m, 2.00 m, 2.01 m → Accurate and precise
  • Temperature measured as 22.0°C, 23.5°C, 21.8°C when actual is 22.0°C → Not precise, partially accurate
  • Time measured with a stopwatch giving 12.5 s repeatedly, but true time is 13.0 s → Precise but not accurate

Key Takeaways

  • Accuracy refers to closeness to the true value; precision refers to repeatability.
  • Measurements can be accurate, precise, both, or neither.
  • Understanding both is critical for reliable experimental results in physics and science.
