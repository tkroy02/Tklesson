Section 8.10: Practice Problems & Revision

This section consolidates key concepts from AC circuits with inductance, capacitance, and RLC series circuits. Attempt the problems to test your understanding and prepare for revision.

Practice Problems

  1. A 0.1 H inductor is connected to a 50 Hz AC supply of 10 V. Calculate the inductive reactance and peak current.
  2. A 20 μF capacitor is connected to 60 Hz AC of 12 V. Find its reactance and peak current.
  3. An RLC series circuit has R = 15 Ω, L = 0.05 H, C = 50 μF, connected to 60 Hz AC, 20 V. Find the total impedance and peak current.
  4. For the RLC circuit above, determine the phase angle and explain whether the current leads or lags the voltage.
  5. Calculate the resonance frequency of an RLC series circuit with L = 0.2 H and C = 25 μF.
  6. Compare energy stored in an inductor (L = 0.1 H, I = 2 A) and in a capacitor (C = 50 μF, V = 10 V).
  7. Explain how the current behaves in purely inductive, purely capacitive, and RLC series circuits in AC.
  8. For an RLC series circuit, calculate the current if frequency is doubled and discuss the effect on impedance.
  9. Describe the phase relationships in a series RLC circuit at resonance and off-resonance.
  10. Sketch voltage and current phasors for a series RLC circuit with R = 10 Ω, L = 0.1 H, C = 50 μF at 50 Hz, and explain.

Example 1

An RLC series circuit has R = 10 Ω, L = 0.1 H, C = 100 μF connected to 50 Hz AC, 12 V. Calculate total impedance, peak current, and phase angle.

Inductive reactance: \( X_L = 2 \pi f L = 2 \pi \cdot 50 \cdot 0.1 \approx 31.42 \, \Omega \)

Capacitive reactance: \( X_C = \frac{1}{2 \pi f C} = \frac{1}{2 \pi \cdot 50 \cdot 100 \times 10^{-6}} \approx 31.83 \, \Omega \)

Impedance: \( Z = \sqrt{R^2 + (X_L - X_C)^2} = \sqrt{10^2 + (31.42 - 31.83)^2} \approx 10 \, \Omega \)

Peak current: \( I_0 = \frac{V_0}{Z} = \frac{12}{10} = 1.2 \, \text{A} \)

Phase angle: \( \phi = \arctan\left(\frac{X_L - X_C}{R}\right) = \arctan\left(\frac{-0.41}{10}\right) \approx -2.35^\circ \) (current slightly leads voltage)
