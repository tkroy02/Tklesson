Section 8.8: AC Circuits with Capacitance

In AC circuits, capacitors oppose changes in voltage, introducing capacitive reactance. This affects the amplitude and phase of current and voltage.

Capacitive Reactance:

\[ X_C = \frac{1}{2 \pi f C} \]

Where \( X_C \) is the reactance, \( f \) is the AC frequency, and \( C \) is the capacitance.

Current in AC Capacitor:

\[ I(t) = \frac{V_0}{X_C} \sin\left(\omega t + \frac{\pi}{2}\right) \]

Current leads voltage by \(90^\circ\) in a purely capacitive AC circuit.

Applications:
  • AC filters and tuning circuits
  • Phase shifting in AC circuits
  • Energy storage in capacitors

Example 1

An AC circuit contains a 10 μF capacitor connected to a 50 Hz supply of 12 V. Find the capacitive reactance and the peak current.

Capacitive reactance: \( X_C = \frac{1}{2 \pi f C} = \frac{1}{2 \pi \cdot 50 \cdot 10 \times 10^{-6}} \approx 318 \, \Omega \)

Peak current: \( I_0 = \frac{V_0}{X_C} = \frac{12}{318} \approx 0.0377 \, \text{A} \)

Practice Problems

  1. A 20 μF capacitor is connected to 60 Hz AC of 24 V. Find its reactance and peak current.
  2. Explain why current leads voltage in a purely capacitive AC circuit.
  3. A capacitor has capacitive reactance 100 Ω at 50 Hz. Find its capacitance.
  4. Describe the effect of increasing frequency on current in an AC capacitive circuit.
  5. For a 50 μF capacitor connected to 100 V, 50 Hz AC, calculate the peak current and phase difference.
