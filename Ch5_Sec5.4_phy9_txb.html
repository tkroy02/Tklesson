5.4 Conservation of Energy

The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another. In a closed system without friction or air resistance, the total energy remains constant.

Key Idea: The sum of kinetic energy (KE) and potential energy (PE) in a system is constant if no external work is done.

Mechanical Energy

Mechanical energy is the sum of an object's kinetic and potential energy:

\[ E_\mathrm{mech} = KE + PE \]

If only conservative forces (like gravity) are acting, mechanical energy is conserved. Friction and air resistance are non-conservative forces that can reduce total mechanical energy.

Example: Rollercoaster

Consider a rollercoaster car of mass 500 kg at the top of a 20 m high hill. Assume friction is negligible. At the top, the car has maximum potential energy and nearly zero kinetic energy.

\[ PE_\mathrm{top} = m g h = (500)(9.8)(20) = 98,000\,\mathrm{J} \]

As the car descends, potential energy is converted into kinetic energy. At the bottom of the hill, the car has maximum kinetic energy and nearly zero potential energy.

\[ KE_\mathrm{bottom} = E_\mathrm{mech} = 98,000\,\mathrm{J} \]

The speed at the bottom can be calculated from kinetic energy:

\[ KE = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 \implies v = \sqrt{\frac{2 KE}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 (98,000)}{500}} \approx 19.8\,\mathrm{m/s} \]

Kinetic and Potential Energy vs. Height

As an object moves in a gravitational field:

  • Potential energy increases with height and decreases as it falls.
  • Kinetic energy increases as speed increases and decreases as speed decreases.
  • The sum of KE and PE remains constant if no energy is lost to friction or air resistance.

Practice Problems

  1. A 2 kg ball is dropped from a height of 5 m. Calculate its potential energy at the top and kinetic energy just before hitting the ground.
  2. A 1000 kg car rolls down a 10 m hill. Neglect friction. Determine its speed at the bottom.
  3. A pendulum with mass 0.5 kg is raised to a height of 1.2 m. Find its PE at the top and KE at the lowest point.

Sample Answers

  1. PE_top = 2*9.8*5 = 98 J; KE_bottom = 98 J; v = √(2*98/2) = 9.9 m/s
  2. PE_top = 1000*9.8*10 = 98,000 J; KE_bottom = 98,000 J; v = √(2*98,000/1000) ≈ 14 m/s
  3. PE_top = 0.5*9.8*1.2 = 5.88 J; KE_bottom = 5.88 J; v = √(2*5.88/0.5) ≈ 4.85 m/s

Check Yourself

A rollercoaster car with a mass of 60 kg travels along a frictionless track. The heights along the journey are: 300 m, 250 m, 200 m, 150 m, 100 m, 75 m, 50 m, 25 m, and 0 m. Assume friction and air resistance are negligible.

Determine for each point:

  1. The gravitational potential energy (PE).
  2. The kinetic energy (KE).
  3. Verify whether mechanical energy is conserved and explain why.

Hint: Use \( PE = m g h \) and \( KE = E_\mathrm{mech} - PE \). Total mechanical energy is the same at all points if no energy is lost to friction or air resistance.

Sample Answers

Assume \( g = 9.8 \, \mathrm{m/s^2} \). Total mechanical energy at the top (300 m):

\( E_\mathrm{mech} = PE_\mathrm{top} + KE_\mathrm{top} = (60)(9.8)(300) + 0 = 176,400 \, \mathrm{J} \)

  1. Height 300 m: PE = 176,400 J, KE = 0 J
  2. Height 250 m: PE = 147,000 J, KE = 29,400 J
  3. Height 200 m: PE = 117,600 J, KE = 58,800 J
  4. Height 150 m: PE = 88,200 J, KE = 88,200 J
  5. Height 100 m: PE = 58,800 J, KE = 117,600 J
  6. Height 75 m: PE = 44,100 J, KE = 132,300 J
  7. Height 50 m: PE = 29,400 J, KE = 147,000 J
  8. Height 25 m: PE = 14,700 J, KE = 161,700 J
  9. Height 0 m: PE = 0 J, KE = 176,400 J

Mechanical energy check: The total mechanical energy remains 176,400 J at every point. This shows that mechanical energy is conserved because there are no non-conservative forces (friction or air resistance) acting on the system.

Check Yourself: Energy Loss & Mechanical Energy

Solve the following problems considering energy loss due to friction or other non-conservative forces.

  1. A 2.0 kg box slides down a ramp from a height of 5.0 m. Due to friction, it loses 15 J of mechanical energy. Calculate:
    • The potential energy at the top.
    • The kinetic energy at the bottom.
    • The total mechanical energy at the bottom.
  2. A 1200 kg car accelerates from rest along a flat road. The engine supplies 50,000 J of work, but friction dissipates 20,000 J as heat. Calculate:
    • The kinetic energy the car would have gained if there were no friction.
    • The actual kinetic energy of the car after accounting for friction.
    • The actual velocity of the car at that point in time.
  3. A rollercoaster of mass 80 kg starts at rest at a height of 50 m. Friction causes it to lose 10% of its total mechanical energy by the time it reaches the bottom. Determine:
    • The total mechanical energy at the top.
    • The kinetic energy at the bottom.
    • Verify whether mechanical energy is fully conserved and explain.

Sample Answers

  1. PE at top: \( PE = m g h = 2.0 \cdot 9.8 \cdot 5.0 = 98 \, \mathrm{J} \)
    KE at bottom: \( KE = PE - \text{energy lost} = 98 - 15 = 83 \, \mathrm{J} \)
    Total mechanical energy at bottom: \( 98 - 15 = 83 \, \mathrm{J} \)
  2. KE without friction: \( KE_\mathrm{ideal} = 50,000 \, \mathrm{J} \)
    Actual KE: \( KE_\mathrm{actual} = 50,000 - 20,000 = 30,000 \, \mathrm{J} \)
    Actual velocity: \( v = \sqrt{\frac{2 \cdot KE_\mathrm{actual}}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{2 \cdot 30,000}{1200}} \approx 7.07 \, \mathrm{m/s} \)
  3. Total mechanical energy at top: \( E_\mathrm{mech} = m g h = 80 \cdot 9.8 \cdot 50 = 39,200 \, \mathrm{J} \)
    KE at bottom: \( KE = 90\% \cdot 39,200 = 35,280 \, \mathrm{J} \)
    Mechanical energy is not fully conserved due to friction; 10% of the energy is lost to non-conservative forces.

Summary

  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.
  • Mechanical energy = KE + PE.
  • In the absence of friction, mechanical energy is conserved.
  • KE and PE continuously transform as an object moves in a gravitational field.
  • Rollercoaster problems illustrate the exchange of KE and PE with height.
