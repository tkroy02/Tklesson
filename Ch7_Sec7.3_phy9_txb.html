Section 7.3: Heat Transfer

Heat transfer is the process by which thermal energy moves from a hotter object or region to a cooler one. The method of transfer depends on the medium and situation. The three main mechanisms are conduction, convection, and radiation.

Conduction

Conduction is the transfer of heat through a substance by the collision of particles and vibration of atoms or molecules, without the bulk movement of the material. It occurs in solids, liquids, and gases, but is most efficient in solids because particles are closely packed.

Formula: \( Q = \frac{k A \Delta T t}{d} \)

  • \(Q\) = heat transferred (J)
  • \(k\) = thermal conductivity (W/m·K)
  • \(A\) = cross-sectional area (m²)
  • \(\Delta T\) = temperature difference (K)
  • \(t\) = time (s)
  • \(d\) = thickness of material (m)
Example: A metal rod of area 0.01 m² and length 0.5 m has ends at 100°C and 25°C. Thermal conductivity \(k = 200 W/m·K\). Heat transferred in 10 s:
\( Q = \frac{200 × 0.01 × (100 - 25) × 10}{0.5} = 3000 \, J \)

Examples of Conduction

  1. Heating one end of a metal spoon in a hot cup of tea – the handle eventually gets warm due to conduction.
  2. Ironing clothes – heat from the iron’s plate is conducted to the fabric.
  3. Cooking on a metal pan – heat transfers from the stove to the pan and then to the food.
  4. Touching a metal door handle in winter – heat from your hand is conducted away to the cold metal.
  5. Heating a rod in a physics lab experiment – the flame heats one end, and heat travels along the rod to the other end.

Convection

Convection is the transfer of heat by the bulk movement of fluids (liquids or gases). Warmer, less dense fluid rises while cooler, denser fluid sinks, creating convection currents.

  • Occurs naturally (natural convection) or is assisted using pumps or fans (forced convection).
  • Example: Boiling water in a pot, hot water rises, cooler water sinks.
Example: A heater warms air in a room. Hot air rises near the heater and cooler air moves down to replace it, circulating heat throughout the room.

Examples of Convection

  1. Boiling water – hot water rises and cooler water sinks, forming convection currents in the pot.
  2. Heating a room with a radiator – warm air rises from the radiator, cooler air moves in to replace it.
  3. Sea and land breezes – during the day, land heats faster than sea, causing air to move from sea to land; reverse at night.
  4. Hot air balloon – heated air inside the balloon rises, lifting the balloon upward.
  5. Weather patterns – warm air masses rise, cool, and sink, creating atmospheric convection currents.

Radiation

Radiation is the transfer of heat in the form of electromagnetic waves, primarily infrared. It does not require a medium and can occur through vacuum.

  • Example: Heat from the Sun reaches Earth through space.
  • Objects absorb or emit radiation depending on temperature and surface properties.
Example: A blackened metal plate in sunlight heats faster than a shiny plate because it absorbs more radiant energy.

Examples of Radiation

  1. Sunlight warming the Earth – energy travels through space without a medium.
  2. Feeling heat from a fireplace – infrared radiation transfers energy directly to your skin.
  3. Heat from a light bulb – emitted in the form of infrared radiation.
  4. Microwave oven – radiation heats food by exciting water molecules.
  5. Heat from a campfire – energy radiates outward as infrared waves that warm nearby objects.

Thermal Radiation Details

Thermal radiation is emitted by all objects above absolute zero, but the specific form it takes depends on the object's temperature. While they are all part of the electromagnetic spectrum, they can be categorized by their energy levels and wavelengths.

  • Infrared Radiation: This is the most common form of thermal radiation you encounter daily. Objects at or near room temperature, like a human body, a campfire, or a hot stove, emit most of their thermal energy as infrared waves. This is the heat you feel from these sources.
  • Visible Light: As an object gets hotter, its thermal radiation begins to include visible light. Think of a stove element glowing red-hot or molten metal glowing yellow. The hotter it gets, the more it shifts from infrared into the visible spectrum.
  • Ultraviolet Radiation: At extremely high temperatures (thousands of degrees), an object's thermal radiation can include ultraviolet light. The Sun, for example, emits significant amounts of ultraviolet radiation in addition to visible light and infrared.

Summary

  • Conduction: heat through solids by particle collisions.
  • Convection: heat through fluid motion (liquids/gases).
  • Radiation: heat through electromagnetic waves, no medium needed.
