4.8 Friction and Air Resistance

When two surfaces are in contact, they experience a force that resists their relative motion. This force is called friction. Similarly, when an object moves through the air, it experiences a resistive force called air resistance or drag.

Key Idea: Friction and air resistance always act in the opposite direction to motion.

What is Friction?

Friction occurs because microscopic irregularities on surfaces catch against each other. It depends on:

  • The roughness of the surfaces.
  • The force pressing the surfaces together (normal force).
  • The type of material in contact.

The force of friction can be calculated using:

\[ F_{friction} = \mu \cdot F_{normal} \]

Where:

  • \(F_{friction}\) = Frictional force (N)
  • \(\mu\) = Coefficient of friction (no units)
  • \(F_{normal}\) = Normal force (N)

Types of Friction

  • Static Friction: The force that prevents motion when an object is at rest.
  • Kinetic Friction: The force opposing motion once the object is already moving.
  • Rolling Friction: Resistance when an object rolls over a surface (e.g., wheels).

Air Resistance (Drag)

When an object moves through the air, particles collide with it, creating a force opposite to its motion. This is called air resistance.

  • Depends on the object's shape (streamlined shapes reduce air resistance).
  • Increases with the speed of the object.
  • Is why parachutes slow skydivers and why cars are designed to be aerodynamic.

Direction of Air Resistance

Air resistance is a force that always opposes the direction of motion, so its direction is entirely dependent on the path of the object. Here's how it would act in different scenarios:

  • Upward: When an object is falling, air resistance acts upward to slow its descent..
  • Downward: A rocket accelerating straight up experiences air resistance acting downward against its motion.
  • Left or Right: An object moving to the right will have air resistance pushing to the left. If the object turns and moves to the left, the air resistance will then push to the right.

Example: Car Moving Against Friction and Air Resistance

Imagine a car driving on a flat road.

  • The engine provides a forward driving force.
  • Friction between the tires and the road helps with grip.
  • Air resistance acts backward, opposing the car's motion.

The car moves at constant speed when the forward force equals the combined backward forces of friction and air resistance.

Practice Problems

  1. A 20 kg box is resting on a surface with a coefficient of friction of 0.4. Calculate the frictional force acting on it. (\(g = 9.8 \, m/s^2\))
  2. If a 600 N forward force is applied to a car, but friction and air resistance total 500 N, what is the net force?
  3. Explain why streamlined cars have better fuel efficiency.
  4. A cyclist experiences 50 N of air resistance at a certain speed. If the cyclist doubles their speed, explain what happens to the air resistance.
  5. Distinguish between static and kinetic friction using an example.
