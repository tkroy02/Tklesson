5.8 Efficiency

In physics, efficiency measures how well a machine converts the input energy or work into useful output energy or work. No machine is perfectly efficient because some energy is always lost, usually as heat due to friction.

Key Concept: Efficiency shows the fraction of input energy that is converted to useful work.

Formula for Efficiency

Efficiency is calculated using the formula:

\[ \text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{Useful Work Output}}{\text{Work Input}} \times 100\% \]

  • Useful Work Output = work done on the load (Joules, J)
  • Work Input = total work or energy supplied to the machine (Joules, J)

Efficiency is usually expressed as a percentage. A perfectly efficient machine would have 100% efficiency, but in practice, efficiency is always less than 100%.

Worked Example

A lever is used to lift a load of 200 N a distance of 2 m. The input force is applied over 3 m and requires 750 J of work. Calculate the efficiency of the lever.

Solution:
Useful work output: \( W_\text{out} = F \cdot d = 200 \cdot 2 = 400\,\mathrm{J} \)
Work input: \( W_\text{in} = 750\,\mathrm{J} \)
\[ \text{Efficiency} = \frac{400}{750} \times 100\% = 53.3\% \]
Answer: The lever has an efficiency of 53.3%.

Practice Problems

  1. A pulley system lifts a 500 N load 5 m. If 3000 J of work is done on the system, calculate the efficiency.
  2. A hydraulic press requires 1200 J of input work to lift an 800 N load by 1.5 m. Determine its efficiency.

Sample Answers

  1. Useful work: \( W_\text{out} = 500 \cdot 5 = 2500\,\mathrm{J} \)
    Efficiency: \( \frac{2500}{3000} \times 100\% = 83.3\% \)
  2. Useful work: \( W_\text{out} = 800 \cdot 1.5 = 1200\,\mathrm{J} \)
    Efficiency: \( \frac{1200}{1200} \times 100\% = 100\% \) (ideal case, usually less in reality)

Summary

  • Efficiency measures how well input energy is converted to useful output energy.
  • Efficiency is always less than 100% in real machines due to energy losses (friction, heat).
