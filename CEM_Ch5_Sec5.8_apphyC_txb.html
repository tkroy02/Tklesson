Section 5.8: Distributed Resistance Calculations

For circuits where resistance is distributed along a wire or conductor, we model it as a continuous distribution. Let a wire of length \( L \) have uniform resistivity \( \rho \) and cross-sectional area \( A \).

The differential resistance over a segment \( dx \) is:

\[ dR = \frac{\rho \, dx}{A} \]

Total resistance is obtained by integrating over the length:

\[ R = \int_0^L \frac{\rho \, dx}{A} = \frac{\rho L}{A} \]

Key Points:
  • Useful for wires with varying cross-section or nonuniform resistivity.
  • Current density \( J = I/A \) is often considered constant for uniform wires.
  • Integration is necessary when \( A(x) \) or \( \rho(x) \) varies along the wire.

Example 1

A cylindrical wire of length 2 m has resistivity \( 1.7 \times 10^{-8} \Omega \cdot m \) and diameter 1 mm. Find the total resistance.

Cross-sectional area: \( A = \pi r^2 = \pi (0.0005)^2 \approx 7.854 \times 10^{-7} \, m^2 \)

Resistance: \( R = \frac{\rho L}{A} = \frac{1.7 \times 10^{-8} \cdot 2}{7.854 \times 10^{-7}} \approx 0.043 \, \Omega \)

Practice Problems

  1. A wire of length 5 m has resistivity \( 2 \times 10^{-8} \Omega \cdot m \) and radius 0.002 m. Calculate its resistance.
  2. For a wire with linearly varying diameter along its length, set up the integral for total resistance.
  3. Explain why distributed resistance is important in high-current applications.
  4. Derive the expression for resistance if resistivity varies as \( \rho(x) = \rho_0(1 + \alpha x) \).
  5. Compare distributed resistance with series and parallel resistor models.
