Section 9.5: RL Circuits

An RL circuit consists of a resistor (R) and inductor (L) connected in series with a voltage source. The current changes gradually due to the inductor opposing changes in current.

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law for RL Circuit:

\[ V = L \frac{dI}{dt} + IR \]

Where \( V \) is the applied voltage, \( I \) the current, \( R \) resistance, and \( L \) inductance.

Current Growth in Series RL Circuit:

\[ I(t) = \frac{V}{R} \left( 1 - e^{-t/\tau} \right) \]

Where \( \tau = \frac{L}{R} \) is the time constant of the RL circuit.

Current Decay (Switch Opened):

\[ I(t) = I_0 e^{-t/\tau} \]

Current decays exponentially after removing the voltage source.

Example 1

A series RL circuit has R = 10 Ω, L = 0.2 H, and a voltage of 12 V is applied. Find the current after 0.01 s.

Time constant: \( \tau = L/R = 0.2/10 = 0.02 \, \text{s} \)

Current: \( I(t) = \frac{V}{R} \left( 1 - e^{-t/\tau} \right) = 1.2 \left( 1 - e^{-0.01/0.02} \right) \approx 0.76 \, \text{A} \)

Practice Problems

  1. An RL series circuit has R = 5 Ω, L = 0.1 H, V = 10 V. Calculate the current after 0.02 s.
  2. Determine the time constant of an RL circuit with L = 0.3 H, R = 15 Ω.
  3. Explain how the inductor opposes sudden changes in current.
  4. A 12 V battery is disconnected from an RL circuit with I₀ = 1 A. Write the expression for current decay.
  5. Plot current versus time for a series RL circuit for t = 0 to 5τ.
