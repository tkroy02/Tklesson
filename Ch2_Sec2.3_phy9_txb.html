2.3 Derived Units

Derived units are formed by combining two or more SI base units through multiplication or division to measure physical quantities that cannot be represented by a single base unit alone. These are commonly used in physics, chemistry, and engineering.

For example, speed is measured in meters per second (m/s), which is derived by dividing distance (meters) by time (seconds).

Remember: A derived unit shows the mathematical relationship between physical quantities. For instance, Force is mass × acceleration, so its unit is kg·m/s², which is called a **Newton (N)**.

Common Derived Units

Below is a table of some common derived units you will encounter:

Physical Quantity Formula Derived Unit SI Unit Expression
Area length × length square meter (m²) m × m = m²
Volume length × length × length cubic meter (m³) m × m × m = m³
Speed / Velocity distance ÷ time meter per second (m/s) m ÷ s = m/s
Acceleration velocity ÷ time meter per second squared (m/s²) m ÷ s² = m/s²
Force mass × acceleration Newton (N) kg·m/s²
Pressure force ÷ area Pascal (Pa) N/m² = kg/(m·s²)
Work / Energy force × distance Joule (J) N·m = kg·m²/s²
Power work ÷ time Watt (W) J/s = kg·m²/s³
Density mass ÷ volume kilogram per cubic meter (kg/m³) kg ÷ m³ = kg/m³
Frequency cycles ÷ time Hertz (Hz) 1/s = s⁻¹

Key Takeaways

  • Derived units are combinations of SI base units.
  • They describe quantities such as force, pressure, speed, and energy.
  • Understanding derived units helps in solving real-world problems and interpreting scientific data.
