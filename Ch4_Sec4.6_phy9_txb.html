4.6 Newton's Third Law

Newton's Third Law of Motion states that:

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

This means that forces always come in pairs. When one object applies a force on a second object, the second object applies an equal force in the opposite direction on the first object. These forces are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction.

Key Features of Newton's Third Law

  • The two forces are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.
  • They act on different objects, never on the same object.
  • The forces occur simultaneously — there is no delay between action and reaction.

Examples of Newton's Third Law

  • Walking: Your foot pushes backward on the ground (action). The ground pushes your foot forward (reaction), propelling you forward.
  • Rocket Launch: The rocket pushes exhaust gases downward (action). The gases push the rocket upward (reaction).
  • Jumping Off a Boat: When you jump forward, you push the boat backward. The boat moves in the opposite direction.
  • Bird in Flight: A bird pushes air down with its wings, and the air pushes the bird up.
  • A fish in Motion: A fish pushes water backward with its fins; the water pushes the fish forward.

Common Misconceptions

  • Forces cancel out: Action and reaction forces do not cancel because they act on different objects.
  • Only one object moves: Both objects are affected, though movement depends on their masses. For example, when you push a wall, the wall pushes back, but it doesn’t move due to its large mass and fixed position.

Practice Problems

  1. A person jumps off a boat and moves forward. Why does the boat move backward?
  2. Explain how a bird flies using Newton's third law.
  3. A swimmer pushes the water backward. Describe the action and reaction forces in this case.
  4. Why can a rocket move in space even though there is no air to push against?
  5. Explain why it hurts when you punch a wall with your fist.
