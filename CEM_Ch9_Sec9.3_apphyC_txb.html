Section 9.3: Inductors & Inductance

An inductor is a coil of wire that resists changes in current. The property that quantifies this ability is called inductance. Inductors store energy in their magnetic field.

Inductance (L):

\[ L = \frac{N \Phi_B}{I} \]

Where \( N \) is the number of turns, \( \Phi_B \) the magnetic flux, and \( I \) the current.

Induced EMF in an Inductor:

\[ \mathcal{E} = - L \frac{dI}{dt} \]

The induced EMF opposes the change in current, consistent with Lenz’s Law.

Energy Stored in Inductor:

\[ U = \frac{1}{2} L I^2 \]

Energy is stored in the magnetic field of the inductor.

Example 1

An inductor of 0.2 H carries a current increasing at 3 A/s. Find the induced EMF and energy stored when current is 5 A.

Induced EMF: \( \mathcal{E} = -L \frac{dI}{dt} = -0.2 \cdot 3 = -0.6 \, \text{V} \)

Energy stored: \( U = \frac{1}{2} L I^2 = 0.5 \cdot 0.2 \cdot 5^2 = 2.5 \, \text{J} \)

Practice Problems

  1. An inductor of 0.1 H has a current increasing at 4 A/s. Find the induced EMF.
  2. Calculate the energy stored in a 0.05 H inductor carrying 2 A.
  3. A coil of 100 turns and area 0.01 m² experiences a flux of 0.05 Wb when current is 0.5 A. Find the inductance.
  4. Explain why the induced EMF always opposes current change in an inductor.
  5. An RL circuit has L = 0.2 H, R = 10 Ω, and I = 0 initially. If voltage is suddenly applied, describe current growth.
