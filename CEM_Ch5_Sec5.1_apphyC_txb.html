Section 5.1: Current & Current Density

Electric Current is the rate of flow of charge through a surface. Its SI unit is the Ampere (A).

Current Density represents how current is distributed over a cross-sectional area:

\[ \vec{J} = \frac{I}{A} \hat{n} \]

where \( I \) is the current, \( A \) the area, and \( \hat{n} \) the normal direction of the surface.

Key Points:
  • Current is positive when charges move in the direction of conventional current (positive charges).
  • For a uniform conductor: \( I = nqv_d A \), where \( n \) is carrier density, \( q \) charge, \( v_d \) drift velocity.
  • Current density vector points along the direction of charge flow.

Example 1

Calculate the current in a wire with cross-sectional area 2 mm², carrying electrons at drift speed \( 1 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{m/s} \), with \( n = 8.5 \times 10^{28} \, \text{m}^{-3} \).

\[ I = nqv_d A = (8.5 \times 10^{28})(1.6 \times 10^{-19})(1 \times 10^{-3})(2 \times 10^{-6}) \]

\[ I \approx 2.72 \, \text{A} \]

Practice Problems

  1. Define electric current and its SI unit.
  2. Explain the physical meaning of current density.
  3. Given a wire with area 1 mm² and drift velocity \( 10^{-4} \, \text{m/s} \), calculate the current if \( n = 10^{28} \, \text{m}^{-3} \).
  4. Describe the relation between current and charge carrier density.
  5. Explain why current density is a vector.
