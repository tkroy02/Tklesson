5.5 The Interrelationship between Work, Energy, and Power

In physics, energy is required for work to be done. Work occurs when a force causes a displacement, and the energy needed for this work can come from kinetic, potential, or other forms of energy.

Key Idea: The changes in kinetic energy (KE) and potential energy (PE) of an object reveal the type of motion or forces acting on it.

Energy and Motion Patterns

  • Loss of KE: When an object slows down, its kinetic energy decreases.
  • Gain of KE (PE constant or zero): Horizontal motion; the object speeds up along a flat surface.
  • Gain of KE and loss of PE: Vertical fall; the object converts potential energy into kinetic energy as it descends.
  • Gain of PE and loss of KE: Rise under freefall conditions; energy is converted from kinetic to potential as the object slows upward.
  • Gain of both KE and PE: Indicates a propulsive or sustained force acting on the object, causing it to rise and accelerate simultaneously.
  • KE = 0 and PE = 0: No motion; the object is stationary.

Power

Power measures the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. It tells us how quickly an object gains or loses energy.

\[ P = \frac{W}{t} = \frac{\Delta E}{t} \]

  • P = Power (Watts, W)
  • W = Work done (Joules, J)
  • \(\Delta E\) = Change in energy (Joules, J)
  • t = Time taken (seconds, s)

Power is important in understanding how quickly energy is being used or transferred. For example, a car accelerating faster requires more power, because it gains kinetic energy more rapidly.

Quick Check

For each scenario, describe how KE, PE, and power are involved:

  1. A ball rolls down a frictionless hill from 10 m height.
  2. A car accelerates along a flat road.
  3. A person lifts a weight vertically at constant speed.
  4. A rocket propels upward into the sky.

Sample Answers

  1. KE increases, PE decreases; power depends on rate of descent.
  2. KE increases; PE constant; power depends on acceleration and rate of work.
  3. KE constant (zero if speed constant), PE increases; power depends on lifting speed.
  4. Both KE and PE increase; high power due to sustained propulsive force.

Practice Problems

Analyze the following situations and describe how kinetic energy (KE), potential energy (PE), and total mechanical energy (ME) change. Consider the presence or absence of non-conservative forces like friction or air resistance.

  1. A horizontally moving object slows down due to resistive forces. Describe what happens to KE, PE, and ME.
  2. A horizontally moving object accelerates and moves faster. Describe what happens to KE, PE, and ME.
  3. An object falls vertically in the presence of air resistance. Describe what happens to KE, PE, and ME.
  4. An object falls vertically in a vacuum (no air resistance). Describe what happens to KE, PE, and ME.
  5. An object is thrown upward but slows down before reaching maximum height (air resistance is negligible). Describe what happens to KE, PE, and ME.

Sample Answers

  1. KE decreases, PE = 0, ME decreases due to energy lost to resistive forces.
  2. KE increases, PE = 0, ME increases if additional work is done on the object.
  3. KE increases, PE decreases, ME decreases due to energy lost to air resistance.
  4. KE increases, PE decreases, ME remains constant (energy converts between KE and PE).
  5. KE decreases, PE increases, ME remains constant (energy converts from KE to PE).

Summary

  • Energy is required for work; work transfers energy to an object.
  • Changes in KE and PE reveal the type of motion and forces acting.
  • Power measures the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred: \( P = W/t \).
  • Horizontal motion: KE increases, PE constant; vertical fall: KE increases, PE decreases.
  • Propulsive motion: both KE and PE increase; stationary objects: KE = PE = 0.
