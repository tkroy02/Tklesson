Section 7.2: Heat, Specific Heat, and Latent Heat

Heat is energy transferred between objects due to a temperature difference. The amount of heat depends on the substance's mass, temperature change, and specific heat capacity. In addition to temperature changes, energy can also be absorbed or released during phase changes without changing temperature. This is known as latent heat.

Specific Heat and Heat Capacity

Specific heat capacity is the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of a substance by 1 K (or 1°C). It is an intensive property, meaning it does not depend on the amount of substance.

\( Q = m c \Delta T \)

  • \(Q\) = heat energy (J)
  • \(m\) = mass (kg)
  • \(c\) = specific heat capacity (J/kg·K)
  • \(\Delta T = T_\text{final} - T_\text{initial}\) (K or °C)

Heat capacity, on the other hand, is the quantity of heat required to raise the temperature of the entire object or substance by 1 K (or 1°C). It is an extensive property, meaning it depends on the mass of the object. The relationship is given by:

\( C = m c \)

  • \(C\) = heat capacity of the object (J/K)
  • \(m\) = mass of the object (kg)
  • \(c\) = specific heat capacity of the material (J/kg·K)

Both specific heat and heat capacity explain how heat affects temperature: - A high specific heat means a substance heats up or cools down slowly because it requires more energy per kilogram per degree. - A low specific heat means a substance heats up or cools down quickly. Similarly, a larger mass will require more heat to achieve the same temperature change, which is why heat capacity depends on both mass and material.

Understanding specific heat and heat capacity is essential in real-life applications such as designing thermal systems, cooking, heating buildings, or understanding how oceans moderate climate by storing and releasing heat slowly.

Worked Example 1: Heating a Metal

Calculate the heat required to raise the temperature of 0.8 kg of copper from 25°C to 75°C (c = 385 J/kg·K).

Step 1: Identify values: m = 0.8 kg, c = 385 J/kg·K, ΔT = 75-25 = 50 K

Step 2: Apply formula: \( Q = m c \Delta T = 0.8 × 385 × 50 \)

Step 3: Compute: \( Q = 15,400 \, \text{J} \)

Latent Heat

Latent heat is the energy absorbed or released by a substance during a phase change without any change in temperature. This energy goes into breaking or forming the bonds between particles rather than increasing their kinetic energy. That is why, during melting, boiling, freezing, or condensation, the temperature of a substance remains constant even though heat is being transferred.

The concept of latent heat helps explain many natural and industrial processes, such as ice melting in drinks, boiling water to produce steam, or the heat released during the condensation of water vapor in the atmosphere. It is called “latent” because it is hidden; the energy does not manifest as a temperature change but is stored in the change of phase.

  • Fusion (Melting/Freezing): Fusion refers to the process in which a solid changes into a liquid (melting) or a liquid changes into a solid (freezing). During melting, the substance absorbs energy to overcome the forces holding its particles in a rigid structure. During freezing, the same amount of energy is released as the particles settle into a fixed arrangement.
    Formula: \( Q = m L_f \)
    \(L_f\) = latent heat of fusion (J/kg)
    Example: Melting 1 kg of ice at 0°C requires \( Q = 1 × 334,000 = 334,000 \, J \).
  • Vaporization (Boiling/Condensation): Vaporization occurs when a liquid turns into a gas (boiling or evaporation), and condensation is the reverse process where a gas turns into a liquid. The energy absorbed during vaporization is used to separate particles far enough to enter the gaseous phase, which requires much more energy than melting due to the stronger intermolecular forces in liquids. During condensation, this energy is released to the surroundings.
    Formula: \( Q = m L_v \)
    \(L_v\) = latent heat of vaporization (J/kg)
    Example: Vaporizing 0.5 kg of water at 100°C requires \( Q = 0.5 × 2,260,000 = 1,130,000 \, J \).

Understanding latent heat is essential for engineering applications, such as designing heating and cooling systems, predicting weather patterns, and studying energy transfer in phase-change materials. It also explains why sweating cools the human body: the heat required to evaporate water from the skin removes thermal energy, lowering body temperature. Similarly, the energy released during condensation warms the air, contributing to phenomena like fog or cloud formation.

In summary, latent heat demonstrates that energy transfer can occur without a temperature change, highlighting the difference between heat that changes the state of matter and heat that changes the temperature.

Worked Example 2: Melting Ice

Calculate the energy required to melt 0.5 kg of ice at 0°C. (Lf = 334,000 J/kg)

Step 1: Identify values: m = 0.5 kg, Lf = 334,000 J/kg

Step 2: Apply formula: \( Q = m L_f = 0.5 × 334,000 \)

Step 3: Compute: \( Q = 167,000 \, \text{J} \)

Worked Example 3: Boiling Water

Calculate the energy required to convert 0.2 kg of water at 100°C to steam at 100°C. (Lv = 2,260,000 J/kg)

Step 1: Identify values: m = 0.2 kg, Lv = 2,260,000 J/kg

Step 2: Apply formula: \( Q = m L_v = 0.2 × 2,260,000 \)

Step 3: Compute: \( Q = 452,000 \, \text{J} \)
