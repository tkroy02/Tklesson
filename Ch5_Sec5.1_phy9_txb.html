5.1 Work

In physics, work is done when a force is applied to an object and the object moves in the direction of the force. Work involves both force and displacement. If there is no movement, no work is done, no matter how much force is applied.

Key Concept: For work to occur, a force must cause a displacement in the same or partially same direction.

Example: If you push hard against a wall but the wall doesn’t move, you are not doing any physical work in the physics sense—even if you feel tired!

Work is done only when the object moves.

Formula for Work

The formula for calculating work is:

\[ W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos\theta \]

  • W = Work done (Joules, J)
  • F = Force applied (Newtons, N)
  • d = Displacement caused (meters, m)
  • θ = Angle between force and displacement

If the force is in the exact same direction as the displacement, \( \cos\theta = 1 \), simplifying the equation to \( W = F \cdot d \).

Example Problem

A person pushes a box with a force of 50 N, causing it to move 3.0 m across a smooth floor. The force is applied horizontally.

Solution:
Given: \( F = 50 \, \mathrm{N} \), \( d = 3.0 \, \mathrm{m} \), \( \theta = 0^\circ \)
\[ W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos(0^\circ) = (50)(3.0)(1) = 150 \, \mathrm{J} \] Answer: The work done is 150 J.

Positive and Negative Work

Work can be positive or negative depending on the direction of the force relative to the displacement:

  • Positive Work: Force and displacement are in the same direction.
    Example: Pushing a box forward while it moves forward.
  • Negative Work: Force and displacement are in opposite directions.
    Example: Friction opposing the motion of a sliding object.

Positive vs Negative Work

Zero Work

If the force applied is perpendicular to the displacement, no work is done.

Example: Carrying a heavy backpack while walking horizontally. The upward force from your hand is perpendicular to your horizontal displacement.

\[ W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos(90^\circ) = 0 \]

Try It Yourself

Calculate the work done in the following scenario:

A student pulls a sled with a force of 40 N at an angle of 30° to the horizontal. The sled moves 5.0 m. How much work is done?

Solution:
Given: \( F = 40 \, \mathrm{N} \), \( d = 5.0 \, \mathrm{m} \), \( \theta = 30^\circ \)
\[ W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos(30^\circ) \] \[ W = (40)(5.0)(0.866) = 173.2 \, \mathrm{J} \] Answer: 173 J

Summary

  • Work occurs when a force causes a displacement.
  • The formula for work is \( W = F \cdot d \cdot \cos\theta \).
  • Positive work adds energy to a system, negative work removes energy.
  • No work is done when force is perpendicular to displacement.

Practice Problems

In each of the following, state:

  1. Who/what does the positive work.
  2. Who/what does the negative work.

Note: If no one does positive or negative work, please indicate clearly in your solution. If two or more forces do positive or negative work, please indicate too.

  1. A student pushes on a stationary box, accelerating it across the floor.
  2. A person lifts a bucket from the ground to the shelf.
  3. A car slows down on a flat road due to friction.
  4. Peter carries a heavy suitcase while walking across a flat floor. No vertical motion detected.
  5. A cyclist pedals uphill on a mountain road, overcoming gravitational pull.
  6. A parachutist falls at a constant speed due to gravity and air resistance.
  7. A person holds a suitcase stationary while standing on the ground.
  8. A ball is dropped and falls toward the ground through air-filled space.
  9. A rocket launches and accelerates upward, overcoming gravity.
  10. A student pushes a cart up a ramp and friction slows it down.

Sample Answers

  1. Positive: Student — Negative: Friction
  2. Positive: Person — Negative: Gravity
  3. Positive: None — Negative: Friction
  4. Positive: None — Negative: None
  5. Positive: Cyclist — Negative: Gravity and Friction
  6. Positive: Gravity — Negative: Air Resistance
  7. Positive: None — Negative: None
  8. Positive: Gravity — Negative: Air Resistance
  9. Positive: Rocket engine (exhaust gases) — Negative: Gravity and Air Resistance
  10. Positive: Student — Negative: Friction and Gravity
