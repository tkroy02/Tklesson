4.7 Weight and Gravity

Gravity is the natural force that attracts objects towards one another. On Earth, gravity pulls all objects toward the center of the planet. This force gives objects weight.

Key Concept: Weight is the force of gravity acting on an object's mass.

Weight vs. Mass

  • Mass: A measure of how much matter an object contains. It is a scalar quantity and remains the same regardless of location. Measured in kilograms (kg).
  • Weight: A measure of the gravitational force acting on an object. It is a vector quantity and can change with location (e.g., Earth vs. Moon). Measured in newtons (N).

The relationship between weight and mass is given by the formula:

\[ W = m \cdot g \]

Where:

  • \(W\) = Weight (N)
  • \(m\) = Mass (kg)
  • \(g\) = Acceleration due to gravity (m/sÂ²), on Earth \(g = 9.8 \, m/s^2\).

Worked Example

Problem: A person has a mass of 70 kg. Calculate their weight on Earth.

Solution:

Using the formula: \[ W = m \cdot g \]

Substituting the values: \[ W = 70 \, kg \times 9.8 \, m/s^2 \]

\[ W = 686 \, N \]

Final Answer: The person's weight is 686 N.

Real-Life Examples

  • A 1 kg object weighs 9.8 N on Earth but only 1.6 N on the Moon because the Moon's gravity is weaker.
  • A scale measures your weight, which is the gravitational pull of Earth on your body.
  • Satellites stay in orbit due to the gravitational attraction between Earth and the satellite.

Practice Problems

  1. Calculate the weight of a 45 kg object on Earth.
  2. If the same 45 kg object were on the Moon where \(g = 1.6 \, m/s^2\), what would its weight be?
  3. A bag of rice weighs 98 N. What is its mass?
  4. Explain why astronauts appear weightless while orbiting Earth.
  5. Why does your mass remain the same on different planets, but your weight changes?
