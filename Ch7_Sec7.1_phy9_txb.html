Section 7.1: Temperature and Thermal Energy

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of particles in a substance. Thermal energy is the total internal energy of a system due to the motion of its particles. While temperature measures “hotness,” thermal energy depends on both temperature and the amount of substance.

Note: Temperature is not the same as thermal energy. A small cup of hot water may feel hot (high temperature) but contains much less thermal energy than a large swimming pool at a lower temperature. Thermal energy is extensive (depends on amount), whereas temperature is intensive (independent of amount). Heat flows naturally from higher temperature to lower temperature until thermal equilibrium is reached. Temperature affects the kinetic energy of molecules; thermal energy also includes the sum of all kinetic and potential energies of particles.

Temperature Scales

  • Celsius (°C): This is based on the freezing (0°C) and boiling (100°C) points of water at standard pressure.


  • Kelvin (K): This is the S.I. unit of measuring temperature. Absolute scale; 0 K is absolute zero where particles have minimal motion.
    Conversion from Celsius to Kelvin:
    \( K = X°C + 273.15 \)
    (Where X°C is the temperature in Celsius)

    Reverse conversion from Kelvin to Celsius:
    \( °C = YK - 273.15 \)
    (Where YK is the temperature in Kelvin)


  • Fahrenheit (°F): Used mainly in the US.
    Conversion from Celsius to Fahrenheit:
    \( °F = \frac{9}{5} X°C + 32 \),
    (Where X°C is the temperature in Celsius)

    Reverse conversion from Fahrenheit to Celsius:
    \( °C = \frac{5}{9} (Y°F - 32) \),
    (Where Y°F is the temperature in Fahrenheit)

Worked Examples: Temperature Conversion

Example 1: Convert 25°C to Fahrenheit.

Solution: \(°F = (9/5) × 25 + 32 = 45 + 32 = 77°F\)

Example 2: Convert 98.6°F to Celsius.

Solution: \(°C = (98.6 - 32) × 5/9 = 66.6 × 5/9 ≈ 37°C\)

Example 3: Convert -10°C to Kelvin.

Solution: \(K = -10 + 273.15 = 263.15 K\)

Example 4: Convert 300 K to Celsius.

Solution: \(°C = 300 - 273.15 = 26.85°C\)

Thermal Energy

The thermal energy absorbed or released by a substance can be calculated using the formula:

\( Q = m c \Delta T \)

  • \(Q\) = heat energy (Joules)
  • \(m\) = mass (kg)
  • \(c\) = specific heat capacity (J/kg·K)
  • \(\Delta T = T_\text{final} - T_\text{initial}\) (K or °C)

Worked Example: Heating Water

Calculate the energy required to heat 0.5 kg of water from 20°C to 80°C. (Specific heat of water \(c = 4186 \, \text{J/kg·K}\))

Step 1: Identify the known values:

  • m = 0.5 kg
  • c = 4186 J/kg·K
  • \(T_i = 20°C, T_f = 80°C → ΔT = 60 K\)

Step 2: Apply the formula \(Q = m c ΔT\)

Step 3: Compute: \(Q = 0.5 × 4186 × 60 ≈ 125,580 \, \text{J} ≈ 1.26 × 10^5 \, \text{J}\)
