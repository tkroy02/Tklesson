4.1 Introduction to Forces

A force is a push or a pull that can change an object’s state of motion or shape. Forces are vectors—they have both magnitude and direction. The SI unit of force is the newton (N).

Key Idea: If the net force on an object is zero, its motion does not change (it stays at rest or keeps moving at constant velocity). If the net force is not zero, the object accelerates.

What Can Forces Do?

  • Change speed: speed up or slow down (acceleration or deceleration).
  • Change direction: cause turning or curving paths.
  • Change shape: stretch, compress, bend, or deform objects.

Types of Forces (with everyday examples)

  • Weight (Gravitational force): The Earth pulls objects downward. \( W = mg \), where \( g \approx 9.8 \,\text{m s}^{-2} \).
  • Normal force: The support force from a surface, acting perpendicular to the surface.
  • Friction: A force that opposes motion between surfaces in contact. Often modeled as \( F_f = \mu N \).
  • Air resistance (drag): A resistive force due to motion through air.
  • Tension: The pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, or cable.
  • Spring force: A restoring force from a stretched/compressed spring. \( F_s = kx \).
  • Magnetic and Electrostatic forces: Non-contact forces between magnets or charged objects.
  • Applied force: Any push or pull exerted by a person or another object.
Contact vs. Non-contact: Friction, normal, tension, and spring forces require contact. Gravitational, magnetic, and electrostatic forces act at a distance (no direct contact).

Free-Body Diagrams (FBDs)

A free-body diagram is a sketch showing an object isolated with all forces acting on it as arrows. Arrow length represents magnitude; arrow direction shows the direction of the force.

  • Identify the object of interest.
  • Draw and label all external forces (e.g., \( W, N, F_f, T \)).
  • Use the diagram to find the net force and predict the motion.
Looking ahead: Newton’s Laws relate forces to motion. In particular, Newton’s Third Law: for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Everyday Connections

  • Walking: You push backward on the ground (friction pushes you forward).
  • Seatbelts: Provide a force to reduce your motion during sudden stops.
  • Elevators: Changing normal force changes how “heavy” you feel.
