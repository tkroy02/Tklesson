Section 8.8: Turbulent Flow and Applications

Turbulent flow occurs when fluid moves chaotically, with eddies and mixing, usually at high velocities or with low-viscosity fluids. Unlike laminar flow, turbulent flow is irregular and unpredictable.

Reynolds Number (Re): \[ Re = \frac{\rho v D}{\eta} \] Where:
  • \(\rho\) = fluid density
  • v = velocity of fluid
  • D = characteristic length (pipe diameter)
  • \(\eta\) = dynamic viscosity

Flow is typically laminar if \(Re < 2000\) and turbulent if \(Re > 4000\).

Applications:
  • Pipes, blood flow, and ventilation systems require understanding flow type.
  • Turbulent flow increases mixing and enhances heat or mass transfer.
  • Design of pipelines and hydraulic systems depends on whether flow is laminar or turbulent.

Example 1

Water (\(\rho = 1000 \text{ kg/m³}\), \(\eta = 0.001 \text{ Pa·s}\)) flows in a pipe of diameter 0.05 m at 1 m/s. Determine if flow is laminar or turbulent.

\(Re = \frac{1000 * 1 * 0.05}{0.001} = 50,000\) → Turbulent flow

Example 2

Air flows through a ventilation duct of diameter 0.2 m at 2 m/s. Density 1.2 kg/m³, viscosity 1.8×10⁻⁵ Pa·s. Determine flow type.

\(Re = \frac{1.2 * 2 * 0.2}{1.8e-5} \approx 26,667\) → Turbulent flow
