5.2 Energy and Kinetic Energy

In physics, energy is the ability to do work or cause change. Energy allows objects to move, heat up, light up, or interact in many ways. It exists in many different forms, but the total energy of a closed system is always conserved.

Key Idea: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or transformed from one form to another.

Types of Energy

There are many forms of energy. Some of the most common include:

  • Kinetic Energy (KE): Energy of motion, possessed by moving objects.
  • Potential Energy (PE): Stored energy due to position or condition, like a stretched spring or raised object.
  • Thermal Energy: Energy associated with heat and the movement of particles.
  • Chemical Energy: Stored in the bonds of molecules, released in chemical reactions.
  • Electrical Energy: Carried by moving electric charges.
  • Radiant Energy: Energy of electromagnetic waves, such as light.

In this chapter, we focus on mechanical energy, which includes kinetic and potential energy. These are the forms of energy most directly connected to motion and forces.

Kinetic Energy

Kinetic energy is the energy an object has because of its motion. The faster an object moves and the more mass it has, the more kinetic energy it possesses.

Key Idea: Moving objects can do work because they have kinetic energy.

Formula for Kinetic Energy

The kinetic energy of an object is calculated using the formula:

\[ KE = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 \]

  • KE = kinetic energy (Joules, J)
  • m = mass (kilograms, kg)
  • v = speed (metres per second, m/s)

Because of the \(v^2\) term, small increases in speed lead to much larger increases in kinetic energy.

Worked Example

A 2.0 kg ball rolls with a speed of 3.0 m/s. Calculate its kinetic energy.

Solution:
\[ KE = \tfrac{1}{2} m v^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(2.0)(3.0)^2 = 1.0 \times 9.0 = 9.0\,\mathrm{J} \] Answer: 9.0 J

Kinetic Energy & Work

When net work is done on an object, its kinetic energy changes. This connection is formalized in the work–energy theorem:

\[ W_{net} = \Delta KE = KE_{final} - KE_{initial} \]

Positive net work increases kinetic energy (speeds the object up). Negative net work decreases kinetic energy (slows the object down).

Quick Check

Which of the following increases an object's kinetic energy?

  1. Doubling the mass while keeping speed constant.
  2. Doubling the speed while keeping mass constant.

Answer: Both increase KE, but doubling the speed increases KE by a factor of four (because of the square).

Hint: Mechanical Energy

Mechanical energy is the sum of an object's kinetic and potential energy:

\[ E_{mech} = KE + PE \]

In many problems (when friction is negligible) mechanical energy is conserved: energy can change from kinetic to potential and back again, but the total remains the same. We'll study potential energy and energy conservation in the next section.

Practice Problems

  1. A 0.50 kg ball is thrown and moves at 10 m/s. Calculate its kinetic energy.
  2. A 1000 kg car accelerates from 0 to 20 m/s. How much kinetic energy does it gain?

Answers

  1. \( KE = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.50)(10)^2 = 25\,\mathrm{J} \)
  2. \( KE = \tfrac{1}{2}(1000)(20)^2 = 200\,000\,\mathrm{J} \)

Summary

  • Energy is the ability to do work or cause change.
  • Kinetic energy is the energy of motion: \( KE = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \).
  • KE depends strongly on speed because of the \( v^2 \) term.
  • Net work changes an object's kinetic energy (work–energy theorem).
  • Mechanical energy is the sum of kinetic and potential energy; we'll cover potential energy next.
