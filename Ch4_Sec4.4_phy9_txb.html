4.4 Newton's Second Law of Motion

Newton's second law explains how the motion of an object changes when a net force acts on it. It establishes the relationship between force, mass, and acceleration.

Key Idea: The acceleration of an object depends directly on the net force acting on it and inversely on its mass.

The Law Stated

Newton's second law states:

"The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. The direction of the acceleration is the same as that of the net force."
Mathematical Form: \[ F_\text{net} = m \cdot a \] where:
  • \(F_\text{net}\) = net force (in newtons, N)
  • \(m\) = mass of the object (in kilograms, kg)
  • \(a\) = acceleration of the object (in meters per second squared, m/s²)

Important Points

  • The larger the force, the greater the acceleration for the same mass.
  • The greater the mass, the smaller the acceleration for the same force.
  • Force and acceleration are vector quantities, meaning they have both magnitude and direction.

Example Problem

A 5 kg object is acted on by a net force of 20 N. Find its acceleration.

Solution: \[ F_\text{net} = m \cdot a \] Substituting values: \[ 20 = 5 \cdot a \] \[ a = \frac{20}{5} = 4 \, \mathrm{m/s^2} \] Answer: The object accelerates at \(4 \, \mathrm{m/s^2}\).

Practice Problems

Solve the following problems. Show your steps and include units in your answers.

  1. A 10 kg box is pushed with a net force of 50 N. Find its acceleration.
  2. If a 3 kg ball accelerates at 6 m/s², what is the net force acting on it?
  3. A net force of 200 N causes a car to accelerate at 0.5 m/s². What is the mass of the car?
  4. A 15 kg sled experiences a net force of 45 N to the right. Determine the acceleration and state its direction.
  5. A toy car with a mass of 0.8 kg accelerates at 2.5 m/s². Find the net force required.

Real-Life Application

When you push a shopping cart, a stronger push (greater force) results in greater acceleration. Similarly, a heavy cart requires more force to achieve the same acceleration as a lighter one.
