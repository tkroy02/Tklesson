Section 8.6: Introduction to Sound

Sound is a mechanical wave that travels through a medium (solid, liquid, or gas) due to the vibration of particles. Unlike electromagnetic waves, sound cannot travel through a vacuum because it requires a material medium to propagate.

The Nature of Sound

Sound is produced when an object vibrates, creating compressions and rarefactions in the surrounding medium. These pressure variations travel as longitudinal waves, carrying energy from the source to the listener.

Characteristics of Sound

  • Frequency (f): Determines the pitch of the sound. Higher frequency = higher pitch.
  • Wavelength (λ): The distance between two successive compressions or rarefactions.
  • Amplitude: Related to the loudness of the sound; larger amplitude = louder sound.
  • Speed: Depends on the medium; faster in solids, slower in gases.

Speed of Sound

The speed of sound varies depending on the medium's density and elasticity. Approximate values at 20°C:

  • Air: 343 m/s
  • Water: 1482 m/s
  • Steel: 5960 m/s

Denser and more elastic media generally transmit sound faster.

Applications of Sound

  • Communication: human speech, telecommunication.
  • Medical imaging: ultrasound.
  • Navigation: sonar in submarines.
  • Musical instruments: creating various pitches and tones.
