Section 7.4: Phase Changes

Phase changes occur when a substance transforms from one state of matter to another: solid, liquid, or gas. These changes involve energy transfer, either absorbed or released, but the temperature of the substance remains constant during the transition. Understanding phase changes is critical in thermodynamics, material science, and daily phenomena like melting ice or boiling water.

Types of Phase Changes

  • Melting (Fusion): Solid → Liquid. Energy is absorbed to break molecular bonds. Temperature remains constant.
  • Freezing: Liquid → Solid. Energy is released as molecular motion decreases and bonds form.
  • Vaporization (Boiling/Evaporation): Liquid → Gas. Requires energy input to overcome intermolecular forces.
  • Condensation: Gas → Liquid. Energy is released as gas particles form intermolecular bonds.
  • Sublimation: Solid → Gas. Direct transition without becoming liquid, e.g., dry ice.
  • Deposition: Gas → Solid. Direct transition without becoming liquid, e.g., frost formation.

Energy Involved

The energy absorbed or released during a phase change is called latent heat. Unlike sensible heat, latent heat does not change the temperature of the substance but changes the internal energy associated with molecular arrangements.

  • Latent heat of fusion (\(L_f\)): Energy to melt 1 kg of solid → liquid or freeze 1 kg liquid → solid.
    Formula: \( Q = m L_f \)
  • Latent heat of vaporization (\(L_v\)): Energy to vaporize 1 kg of liquid → gas or condense 1 kg gas → liquid.
    Formula: \( Q = m L_v \)

Worked Examples

Example 1: How much energy is required to melt 0.2 kg of ice at 0°C? (Given \(L_f = 334,000 \, \text{J/kg}\))

Solution: \( Q = m L_f = 0.2 × 334,000 = 66,800 \, \text{J} \)

Example 2: How much energy is released when 0.5 kg of steam condenses to water at 100°C? (Given \(L_v = 2.26 × 10^6 \, \text{J/kg}\))

Solution: \( Q = m L_v = 0.5 × 2.26 × 10^6 = 1.13 × 10^6 \, \text{J} \)
