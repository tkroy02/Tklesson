2.7 Volume and Density

Volume is the amount of space occupied by an object. It is measured in cubic units, typically in the SI system as cubic meters (m³) or more practical units like cm³, mL, L.

Key Concept: Volume can be measured directly using a measuring cylinder for liquids, or calculated for regular solids using geometry.

Measuring Volume

  • Regular solids: Use geometric formulas.
    • Cube: \( V = a^3 \)
    • Rectangular prism: \( V = l \times w \times h \)
    • Sphere: \( V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \)
    • Cylinder: \( V = \pi r^2 h \)
  • Irregular solids: Use water displacement method.

    Submerge the object in a graduated cylinder and measure the rise in water level: \( V_\text{object} = V_\text{final} - V_\text{initial} \)

  • Liquids: Use a measuring cylinder, pipette, or volumetric flask for accurate volume measurement.

Density

Density is a measure of how much mass is contained in a unit volume of a substance. It is an intensive property and can be used to identify substances.

Formula:
\[ \rho = \frac{m}{V} \] where:
  • \( \rho \) = density (kg/m³ or g/cm³)
  • m = mass of the object (kg or g)
  • V = volume of the object (m³ or cm³)

Example Calculations

  1. A metal cube has a mass of 540 g and side length 5 cm. Find its density.
    Solution: \( V = 5^3 = 125 \, \text{cm³} \)
    \( \rho = \frac{540}{125} = 4.32 \, \text{g/cm³} \)
  2. A liquid has a mass of 2.5 kg and occupies 2 L. Find the density.
    Solution: Convert volume to m³: \( 2 \, \text{L} = 0.002 \, \text{m³} \)
    \( \rho = \frac{2.5}{0.002} = 1250 \, \text{kg/m³} \)

Applications

  • Determining whether objects float or sink in a fluid (buoyancy).
  • Identifying unknown substances by comparing density values.
  • Designing objects for specific weight-to-volume ratios in engineering.
