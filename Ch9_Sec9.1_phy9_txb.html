Section 9.1: The Nature of Light

Light is a form of energy that can travel through a vacuum as well as through transparent media. It exhibits both wave-like and particle-like properties, a phenomenon known as wave-particle duality.

Wave Nature of Light

Light behaves as an electromagnetic wave. This means it consists of oscillating electric and magnetic fields, perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation.

  • Light waves are transverse waves.
  • It does not require a medium; it can travel through vacuum at the speed of light, \(c = 3.0 \times 10^8 \, \mathrm{m/s}\).
  • Interference and diffraction experiments confirm its wave-like behavior.

Particle Nature of Light

Light also behaves like a stream of particles called photons, each carrying a quantum of energy:

  • Energy of a photon: \(E = h f\), where \(h\) is Planckâ€™s constant (\(6.63 \times 10^{-34} \, \mathrm{Js}\)) and \(f\) is the frequency.
  • Photoelectric effect demonstrates this particle nature: light can eject electrons from a metal surface if the photon energy is sufficient.
  • The particle description explains phenomena that wave theory alone cannot, such as the quantized energy transfer.

Wave-Particle Duality

The dual nature of light is fundamental in modern physics:

  • Wave theory explains reflection, refraction, diffraction, and interference.
  • Particle theory explains the photoelectric effect and Compton scattering.
  • This duality is essential for understanding light in quantum mechanics.

Key Points

  • Light is an electromagnetic wave that can travel through vacuum.
  • It behaves as photons, which are particles carrying quantized energy.
  • Wave-particle duality explains all observed phenomena of light.
  • Speed of light in vacuum is \(c = 3.0 \times 10^8 \, \mathrm{m/s}\).
