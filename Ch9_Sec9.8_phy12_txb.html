Section 9.8: Problem-Solving Strategies

Relativity problems often require careful identification of frames of reference, applying Lorentz transformations, and using relativistic formulas for time, length, and velocity. Here are strategies to solve such problems efficiently.

Key Strategies:
  • Identify the proper inertial frame for each object.
  • Use \( \Delta t' = \gamma (\Delta t - \frac{v \Delta x}{c^2}) \) for time transformations.
  • Use \( L' = L/\gamma \) for length contraction.
  • Apply \( u' = \frac{u+v}{1 + uv/c^2} \) for velocity addition.
  • Double-check units and ensure all velocities are fractions of \(c\) when necessary.
  • Draw spacetime diagrams for visual clarity when needed.

Example 1

A spaceship moving at 0.7c sends a signal at 0.5c relative to the ship. Use velocity addition to find the signal speed relative to Earth.

\( u' = \frac{0.7c + 0.5c}{1 + (0.7)(0.5)} = \frac{1.2c}{1.35} \approx 0.889c \)

Practice Problems

  1. Calculate time dilation for a spacecraft moving at 0.8c for 5 hours as observed from Earth.
  2. Determine contracted length of a 100 m ship moving at 0.9c.
  3. Find relative speed of two spaceships moving towards each other at 0.6c and 0.7c relative to Earth.
  4. Explain why velocities in classical mechanics cannot exceed c.
  5. Draw a spacetime diagram for a light pulse emitted from a moving source.
