Section 6.6: Vertical Circular Motion

Vertical circular motion refers to motion along a circular path in a vertical plane. Important examples include cars going over a hill, vehicles passing through a dip or valley, and rollercoaster loops. In these situations the normal force (contact force from the surface) and gravity combine to provide the net centripetal force.

Key concept: The normal force changes with position on the path. At the top of a circular arc the normal force may decrease (possibly to zero), while at the bottom it increases. The net radial (centripetal) equation depends on whether the centre of curvature is above or below the object.

Forces at the Top of a Circle (crest / loop top)

At the top of a circular arc the weight acts downward and the normal force acts upward. Using the sign convention toward the centre as positive (downward at the top):

  • Sum of radial forces toward centre: \( mg - N = \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \).
  • Rearranged: \( N = mg - \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \).
Interpretation: As speed increases, \( \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \) grows and the normal force \(N\) decreases. If \(N\) reaches zero the object loses contact (it becomes airborne). The critical condition for losing contact is: \[ N = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad mg = \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \quad\Rightarrow\quad v = \sqrt{g r}. \]

Forces at the Bottom of a Circle (valley / loop bottom)

At the bottom of a circular arc the centre of curvature is above the object, so take upward (toward the centre) as positive:

  • Sum of radial forces toward centre: \( N - mg = \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \).
  • Rearranged: \( N = mg + \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \).
Interpretation: The normal force increases with speed. Riders feel heavier at the bottom because \(N\) is larger than the weight \(mg\).

Vertical Loop: Minimum Speed at the Top

For an object to remain in contact at the top of a vertical loop, the normal force must be non-negative. The minimum speed at the top occurs when \(N=0\):

Minimum speed at top of loop to maintain contact: \[ v_\text{min,top} = \sqrt{g r}. \] Designers typically use a safety factor above this speed to ensure the coaster stays in contact comfortably.

Example 1 — Car Cresting a Hill (Find Normal Force)

A small car of mass 800 kg passes over the crest of a rounded hill of radius r = 20 m with speed v = 12 m/s. Determine the normal force \(N\) exerted by the road on the car at the crest. Also state whether the car stays in contact.

Given: \( m = 800 \, \text{kg},\; r = 20 \, \text{m},\; v = 12 \, \text{m/s},\; g = 9.8 \, \text{m/s}^2 \)

Step 1: Formula for normal force at the top:
\( N = mg - \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \)

Step 2: Compute centripetal term:
\( \dfrac{m v^2}{r} = \dfrac{800 \cdot 12^2}{20} = 5760 \, \text{N} \)

Step 3: Compute weight:
\( mg = 800 \cdot 9.8 = 7840 \, \text{N} \)

Step 4: Find normal force:
\( N = 7840 - 5760 = 2080 \, \text{N} \)

Step 5: Check contact:
Since \(N > 0\), the car remains in contact. Critical speed: \( \sqrt{g r} \approx 14.0 \, \text{m/s} \).

Example 2 — Car Passing Through a Dip (Find Normal Force)

A vehicle of mass 1000 kg travels through a valley with radius of curvature r = 15 m at a speed of v = 10 m/s. Calculate the normal force on the vehicle at the lowest point.

Given: \( m = 1000 \, \text{kg},\; r = 15 \, \text{m},\; v = 10 \, \text{m/s} \)

Step 1: Formula for bottom: \( N = mg + \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \)

Step 2: Compute centripetal term: \( \dfrac{1000 \cdot 10^2}{15} \approx 6666.7 \, \text{N} \)

Step 3: Compute weight: \( mg = 1000 \cdot 9.8 = 9800 \, \text{N} \)

Step 4: Find normal force: \( N \approx 9800 + 6666.7 = 16466.7 \, \text{N} \approx 1.65 \times 10^4 \, \text{N} \)

Step 5: Interpretation: Passengers feel heavier at the bottom.

Example 3 — Rollercoaster Loop: Minimum Speed to Maintain Contact at Top

A rollercoaster has a circular loop of radius r = 8 m. Find the minimum speed a car must have at the top of the loop so that it remains in contact.

Step 1: Condition for contact: \( N \ge 0 \). Minimum occurs when \( N = 0 \)

Step 2: Formula: \( mg = \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \Rightarrow v = \sqrt{g r} \)

Step 3: Compute minimum speed: \( v_\text{min} = \sqrt{9.8 \cdot 8} = \sqrt{78.4} \approx 8.85 \, \text{m/s} \)

Step 4: Designers usually use higher speed for safety and comfort.

Example 4 — Apparent Weight (Seat Force) at Bottom of a Loop

A rollercoaster car + rider have combined mass m = 70 kg. At the bottom of a circular section of radius r = 5 m the speed is v = 12 m/s. Calculate the normal force (apparent weight) exerted on the rider.

Given: \( m = 70 \, \text{kg},\; r = 5 \, \text{m},\; v = 12 \, \text{m/s} \)

Step 1: Formula for bottom: \( N = mg + \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \)

Step 2: Compute centripetal term: \( \dfrac{70 \cdot 12^2}{5} = 2016 \, \text{N} \)

Step 3: Compute weight: \( mg = 70 \cdot 9.8 = 686 \, \text{N} \)

Step 4: Find normal force: \( N = 686 + 2016 = 2702 \, \text{N} \approx 2.70 \times 10^3 \, \text{N} \)

Step 5: Apparent weight: \( N / mg \approx 3.94 \) times usual weight (almost 4 g)

Summary and Physical Intuition

  • At the top: \( N = mg - \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \). Large speeds reduce \(N\); if \(v>\sqrt{g r}\) contact is lost.
  • At the bottom: \( N = mg + \dfrac{m v^2}{r} \). Large speeds increase \(N\) and occupants feel heavier.
  • For loops, the key threshold \(v=\sqrt{g r}\) appears repeatedly — it's the speed scale set by gravity and radius.
