Section 6.4: Centripetal Force and Friction

Friction can act as the centripetal force that keeps vehicles moving safely along curves. The maximum speed without slipping depends on the coefficient of friction, the mass of the vehicle, and the radius of the curve.

Key Concept: On flat surfaces, the frictional force itself produces the centripetal force, so \(F_\text{friction} = F_c\). For banked or inclined surfaces, additional forces may contribute.

Formulas for Flat Curves

  • Maximum speed: \( v_\text{max} = \sqrt{\mu g r} \)
  • Centripetal force: \( F_c = \frac{m v^2}{r} \)
  • Maximum frictional force: \( F_\text{friction,max} = \mu m g \)

Example 1: Maximum Speed on a Flat Curve

A car of mass 1000 kg is negotiating a flat curve with a radius of 50 m. The coefficient of friction between the tires and the road is μ = 0.3. Determine the maximum speed \(v_\text{max}\) that the car can maintain without slipping.

Solution: \( v_\text{max} = \sqrt{\mu g r} = \sqrt{0.3 \cdot 9.8 \cdot 50} \approx 12.12 \, \text{m/s} \)

Example 2: Friction Provides the Centripetal Force

An 800 kg vehicle travels along a flat circular curve of radius 40 m at a speed of 10 m/s. Assuming the frictional force provides the necessary centripetal force, calculate the frictional force required to keep the vehicle on the curve.

Solution: \( F_\text{friction} = F_c = \frac{m v^2}{r} = \frac{800 \cdot 10^2}{40} = 2000 \, \text{N} \)

Example 3: Required Coefficient of Friction

A car of mass 1200 kg is negotiating a flat curve of radius 60 m at a speed of 15 m/s. Determine the minimum coefficient of friction μ required to prevent slipping.

Solution: \( \mu = \frac{v^2}{r g} = \frac{15^2}{60 \cdot 9.8} \approx 0.383 \)

Example 4: Maximum Radius for Given Speed

A vehicle travels at a speed of 8 m/s along a flat curve. If the coefficient of friction is μ = 0.4, determine the maximum radius of the curve for which the vehicle will not slip.

Solution: \( r_\text{max} = \frac{v^2}{\mu g} = \frac{8^2}{0.4 \cdot 9.8} \approx 16.33 \, \text{m} \)

Limits of Speed on Flat Curves

On a flat curve, the speed of a vehicle is limited by the maximum frictional force that can act between the tires and the road. If the required centripetal force exceeds this maximum friction, the car will skid outward.

Maximum Speed: The maximum safe velocity is \[ v_\text{max} = \sqrt{\mu g r} \] Beyond this speed, the car cannot maintain the curve and will slip outward.
Minimum Speed: On a flat curve, there is no lower speed limit for maintaining circular motion. A car may travel as slowly as desired (even stop), since friction can act in either direction. Thus, \[ v_\text{min} = 0 \]
Note: On a banked curve, both a minimum and a maximum speed exist, because the banking angle and friction must balance forces inward and outward. This will be studied in more detail in Grade 11.
