Section 10.1: Gauss’s Law for Electricity

Gauss’s Law relates the electric flux through a closed surface to the total charge enclosed by the surface.

Gauss’s Law Formula:

\[ \oint_S \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = \frac{Q_\text{enc}}{\epsilon_0} \]

Here, \( Q_\text{enc} \) is the total charge enclosed by the surface \( S \), and \( \epsilon_0 \) is the vacuum permittivity.

Applications:
  • Uniformly charged sphere (outside: behaves like point charge; inside: \( E \propto r \))
  • Infinite line of charge: \( E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi \epsilon_0 r} \)
  • Infinite plane sheet: \( E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0} \)

Example 1

Find the electric field at a distance r from an infinite line of charge with linear charge density \( \lambda \).

Choose a cylindrical Gaussian surface of radius r and length L around the line. Flux through the curved surface:

\[ \oint \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{A} = E (2 \pi r L) = \frac{\lambda L}{\epsilon_0} \implies E = \frac{\lambda}{2 \pi \epsilon_0 r} \]

Practice Problems

  1. Use Gauss’s Law to find the electric field inside and outside a uniformly charged spherical shell.
  2. Determine the electric field near an infinite plane sheet with surface charge density \( \sigma \).
  3. Find the electric field at a distance r from an infinite line of charge with linear charge density \( \lambda \).
  4. Explain why Gauss’s Law is useful for symmetric charge distributions.
  5. A solid sphere of radius R has a uniform charge density ρ. Find the electric field at r < R and r > R.
