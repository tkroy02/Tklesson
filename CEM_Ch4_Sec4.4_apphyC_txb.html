Section 4.4: Parallel-Plate Capacitors

The parallel-plate capacitor is the simplest capacitor configuration. It consists of two conducting plates of area \(A\), separated by a distance \(d\), with vacuum or dielectric material between them.

Capacitance of a Parallel-Plate Capacitor:

\[ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d} \] for vacuum (or air, approximately).

Here, \(\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{C}^2/(\text{NÂ·m}^2)\) is the permittivity of free space.

With dielectric of constant \(\kappa\): \[ C = \kappa \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d} \]

Key Insights:
  • Capacitance increases with plate area \(A\).
  • Capacitance decreases with larger separation \(d\).
  • Dielectrics increase capacitance by reducing the effective electric field.

Example 1

A parallel-plate capacitor has plates of area \(0.02 \, \text{m}^2\), separation \(2.0 \, \text{mm}\), and vacuum between them. Find its capacitance.

Using formula:

\[ C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d} = \frac{(8.85 \times 10^{-12})(0.02)}{2.0 \times 10^{-3}} \]

\[ C = 8.85 \times 10^{-11} \, \text{F} = 88.5 \, \text{pF} \]

Practice Problems

  1. Derive the expression for capacitance of a parallel-plate capacitor.
  2. A parallel-plate capacitor has \(A = 0.05 \, \text{m}^2\), \(d = 1 \, \text{mm}\). Find \(C\).
  3. If a dielectric of \(\kappa = 4\) is inserted, what happens to capacitance?
  4. Explain why reducing plate separation increases capacitance.
  5. What practical limitations exist when making \(d\) very small?
