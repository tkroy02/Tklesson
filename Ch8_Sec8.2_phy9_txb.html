Section 8.2: Wave Properties

All waves, whether mechanical or electromagnetic, share common measurable properties. These properties describe how waves move, how fast they travel, and how they interact with matter. By understanding these properties, we can better analyze sound, light, and other forms of wave motion.

Wavelength (λ)

The wavelength is the distance between two consecutive points that are “in phase” on a wave. For example: • In a transverse wave, it’s the distance between two crests or two troughs. • In a longitudinal wave, it’s the distance between two compressions or two rarefactions.
Symbol: λ (Greek letter lambda), measured in meters (m).

Frequency (f)

The frequency of a wave is the number of complete cycles (vibrations) that pass a point per second.
Symbol: f, measured in hertz (Hz), where 1 Hz = 1 cycle per second.
Example: A sound wave with a frequency of 440 Hz produces the musical note A above middle C.

Period (T)

The period is the time it takes for one complete wave cycle to pass a given point.
Symbol: T, measured in seconds (s).
Frequency and period are related by:

$$ T = \frac{1}{f} $$

Amplitude (A)

The amplitude of a wave is the maximum displacement of the particles from their rest position. It measures the wave’s energy: the larger the amplitude, the more energy the wave carries.
Example: A loud sound has a higher amplitude than a soft sound.

Wave Speed (v)

The wave speed tells us how fast the disturbance travels through the medium or space. It is related to wavelength and frequency by the equation:

$$ v = f \lambda $$

where \( v \) is wave speed (m/s), \( f \) is frequency (Hz), and \( \lambda \) is wavelength (m).
Example: The speed of sound in air is about 343 m/s at room temperature.
