Section 8.2: Radioactivity

Radioactivity is the spontaneous emission of particles or radiation from unstable nuclei. It is fundamental for understanding nuclear decay processes and applications in energy and medicine.

Types of Radioactive Decay:
  • Alpha (\(\alpha\)) decay: Emission of a helium nucleus.
  • Beta (\(\beta\)) decay: Transformation of a neutron to a proton or vice versa, emitting an electron or positron.
  • Gamma (\(\gamma\)) decay: Emission of high-energy photons.
  • Half-life (\(T_{1/2}\)): Time required for half of a radioactive sample to decay.

Example 1

The half-life of Carbon-14 is 5730 years. How much of a 20 g sample remains after 11460 years?

Number of half-lives = 11460 / 5730 = 2

Remaining mass = \( 20 \times (1/2)^2 = 20 \times 1/4 = 5 \) g

Practice Problems

  1. A 10 g sample of Uranium-238 has a half-life of 4.5 × 10⁹ years. How much remains after 9 × 10⁹ years?
  2. Identify the type of decay in the reaction: \( ^{14}_6C \rightarrow ^{14}_7N + \beta^- \).
  3. Calculate the decay constant for a radioactive isotope with a half-life of 10 days.
  4. Explain why gamma decay does not change the number of protons or neutrons.
  5. A sample of Iodine-131 has a half-life of 8 days. How long until only 12.5% remains?
  6. Describe the difference between alpha and beta radiation in terms of mass and charge.
  7. Calculate the remaining mass of a 50 g Polonium-210 sample after 60 days if \( T_{1/2} = 138 \) days.
  8. Explain why radioactive decay is considered a random process.
  9. Sketch a decay curve for a sample of Cobalt-60.
  10. Discuss one medical application of radioactive isotopes.
